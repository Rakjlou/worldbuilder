# Metro — Lore

## The World As It Was

The world of Metro is our world. No divergence, no alternate history. The starting point is the real Earth at the turn of the 21st century — the Internet age, pre-AI, globally connected but politically fractured. The technology is what we had. The people are who we were.

Everything that follows grows from a single observation made at a telescope.

---

## The Object

An anomaly detected during a routine sky survey. Initially dismissed — a data artifact, a calibration error, a colleague's oversight. But the numbers don't go away. Over weeks, then months, the anomaly resolves into something that should not exist: a massive body on a direct trajectory toward Earth. Not a near-miss. Not a statistical possibility. A certainty.

The object is large. Planet-scale large. It moves fast — fast enough that the window between confirmed detection and projected collision is measured in decades, not centuries. This speed is the master variable of the world: it sets the pace of everything that follows. How much time there is to deny, to study, to argue, to build, to fail.

Early analysis reveals troubling data. The object's composition, its mass, its spectral signature — they match nothing in any catalog. Or rather, they match one thing too well. The scientific evidence pointing to the object's true nature accumulates slowly, reluctantly, like a diagnosis no one wants to confirm.

What is done with this information — who learns it, when, and how the world reacts — is not prescribed. The lore establishes only what is true: the object is real, it is coming, and the data about its nature is available to anyone with the instruments and the will to look.

## Teleportation

### Origin

Teleportation does not exist when the story begins. It is not a background technology. It is not theoretical. It is not "almost there." It is nowhere.

Its development is a direct consequence of the threat. Whether it emerges from a desperate research initiative, a private breakthrough, or an accidental discovery is open — but it is born from the crisis, not predating it.

### The Mechanism

Teleportation operates through paired spatial discontinuities — points in space that are connected such that crossing one means exiting the other. The engineering details are a problem for the builders. The principle is simple: matter enters here, matter exits there.

But the principle has a hidden cost.

### The Temporal Displacement

Every teleportation event produces a negative temporal displacement: the object arrives at its destination fractionally *before* it departed from its origin. At small scales — laboratory experiments, cargo transfers — this displacement is small. Microseconds. Nanoseconds. A measurable oddity, confirmed and reproduced in controlled settings. Who knows about this property, when they learn it, and what is done with that knowledge is not prescribed by the lore.

The relationship between energy and temporal displacement is exponential. The curve is steep: scaling up from lab equipment to industrial cargo barely registers. But at each new order of magnitude, the displacement stretches further. The math is well-characterized. There are models, tables, error margins.

### The Plan

At planetary scale, the temporal displacement is significant — but *calculable*. The entire teleportation project is designed around this constraint. The destination coordinates, the energy budget, the displacement offset: all of it is modeled, reviewed, debated, and built into the system's parameters. The plan accounts for the time shift. The Earth would arrive at a safe location, at a known point in the past, with a trajectory that poses no threat.

On paper, it works. The models agree. The engineering is sound.

The plan assumes the system is activated correctly.

### What Teleportation Is Not

Teleportation is a young technology. It has been scaled up from laboratory curiosities to industrial applications over the course of the crisis, driven by necessity rather than maturity. The planetary system represents a leap of several orders of magnitude beyond anything previously built. It has been modeled, simulated, and tested in every way that can be tested short of full activation.

But it has never been fired at full scale. It cannot be test-fired at full scale. The first planetary activation is the real one.

## The Paradox

The evidence suggests a closed causal loop: the Earth is teleported to escape the approaching object, but the teleportation sends it back in time, making it *become* the approaching object. The threat creates the response that creates the threat.

This is never established as absolute fact within the world. The data is compelling. The models are consistent. But compelling data and consistent models have been wrong before, and the human capacity to reject an intolerable conclusion is vast.

The paradox generates a fracture line through every institution, every team, every relationship:

- **If the loop is real**, then activating the teleporter is what causes the collision. The only way to break the cycle is to not teleport — and accept the impact.
- **If the loop is not real** (or if it can be broken), then the teleporter is humanity's only chance. Not using it means certain death.

There is no way to know which is true without activating the system. And activation is irreversible.

---

## The Planetary Wager

Building a teleportation system capable of moving an entire planet is not a scientific project. It is the largest industrial undertaking in human history.

It requires global coordination — or global coercion, or some unstable mixture of both. It requires energy infrastructure that dwarfs anything ever built. It requires supply chains, labor forces, political agreements, funding mechanisms, and engineering tolerances that push every human system to its limit.

The process by which humanity commits to this path — or has it imposed — is itself a source of drama. The wager is not just scientific. It is political, economic, social, and deeply personal. Entire economies reorient. Careers are built and destroyed. Communities are displaced for construction. The question of whether to build the system is inseparable from the question of who controls it, who profits from it, and who bears the cost.

The system, once built, requires someone to activate it. That activation requires a decision, and that decision exists at the intersection of science, politics, philosophy, and individual human judgment.

---

## The Activation

The lore establishes only this: the system is activated, and it is activated badly. Not as designed. Not as intended. Something goes wrong — something rooted in the human element, not the physics.

Whether the activation is premature, unauthorized, sabotaged, accidental, or simply the result of a cascading series of small human failures is not prescribed. What matters is that the cause is recognizably human. Not a cosmic joke. Not a technical impossibility. Something that, in retrospect, was always going to happen because of who these people were and what they were under pressure to do.

The consequences are planetary and immediate. The surface is devastated. Whether anyone survives, and in what condition, and what they find — that belongs to the story, not the lore.

---

## The Scientific Team

At the center of this world is a small team of scientists — six or seven people — who are present from the early days of the discovery through to the crisis. They are not the only people who matter, but they are the ones closest to the truth, the longest.

They are researchers: astronomers, physicists, engineers, and adjacent disciplines. They are the people who first saw the data, who built the models, who argued about what it meant, who watched the world change around their discovery.

Over the years, their relationships with each other deepen, complicate, and fracture under the weight of what they know. They are colleagues, rivals, friends, former lovers, mentors, and protégés. The interpersonal dynamics within this group create the conditions — directly or indirectly — for how the activation unfolds.

They are not heroes or villains. They are people who happened to be looking at the sky at the right time, and who then had to live with what they saw.

---

## The World Around Them

Beyond the team, the world responds to the threat in every way humans respond to existential pressure:

- **Denial and normalcy**: For years, most people do not know. Then they know but do not believe. Then they believe but continue living.
- **Institutional response**: Governments, agencies, international bodies — slow, political, self-interested, sometimes competent.
- **Industry**: The teleportation project creates its own economy. Contractors, engineers, laborers, investors, profiteers.
- **Media and information**: Leaks, rumors, conspiracy theories, genuine journalism, disinformation. The truth is available but so is everything else.
- **Social fracture**: Those who support the teleporter. Those who oppose it. Those who think it's propaganda. Religious movements. Protest movements. Resignation. Rage.
- **Daily life**: Through all of it, people go to work, raise children, fall in love, get sick, argue about mundane things. The apocalypse is background noise until it isn't.

---

## What the Lore Does Not Decide

The following are deliberately left open for seeds to determine:

- The exact timeline (years between discovery and activation)
- The exact temporal displacement (how far back the Earth is sent)
- Who discovers the object's true nature, and when
- Whether the information is public or secret, and for how long
- The political structure around the teleportation project
- Who controls the activation
- What specifically goes wrong during the activation
- Whether the causal loop has an origin or is truly closed
- What happens after the activation
- Whether anyone on the approaching Earth is alive
- The fate of the characters

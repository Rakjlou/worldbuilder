# Yara Haddad

## Who They Are

Yara Haddad is a forty-one-year-old Jordanian-Canadian systems engineer who has spent the last nine years at CERN, first on the LHC's cryogenics infrastructure, then migrating into the accelerator controls group. Before CERN she did her doctorate at McGill on feedback control systems for high-energy beam optics — the kind of work that involves writing software that prevents billion-dollar magnets from quenching and destroying themselves. She is very good at making complicated things not explode.

She was born in Amman in 1985, moved to Mississauga, Ontario at age seven when her father took a civil engineering post with an infrastructure firm. She has Jordanian and Canadian passports. She speaks Arabic (Levantine dialect, rusty but functional), English (native-fluent, Canadian-inflected), and French (serviceable, daily-use at CERN, grammar crimes she does not care about). She also reads some German from living in the Geneva-Basel corridor, mostly menus and train schedules.

Before the object, Yara was on a three-year contract renewal at CERN, leading the controls integration for the High-Luminosity LHC upgrade — making the new beam optics, cryogenics, and vacuum subsystems talk to each other through a unified control framework. She was content. Not ecstatic, not restless, just occupied with work she found genuinely interesting and a life in Geneva that suited her temperament — small apartment, a decent bike, a gym membership she uses four days a week, a cat named Diesel.

She has no children and has never been married, though she lived with a structural engineer named Luís for four years in her early thirties. They split amicably when he returned to Porto. She sends him a message on his birthday. He sends her photos of his daughter.

## Personality

Yara listens for about thirty seconds before she starts sketching on whatever surface is available — napkin, whiteboard, tablet, the back of someone's printed-out email. She thinks spatially and temporally; she maps relationships between systems before she forms opinions about them.

She does not raise her voice in meetings. When she disagrees, she asks questions — pointed ones, with specific numbers in them. "What's your margin on the thermal load at sixty percent capacity?" is how Yara says "I think you're wrong." If you answer well, she nods and moves on. If you answer badly, she asks another question.

She is patient with complexity and impatient with imprecision. She will spend four hours helping a junior engineer debug a timing issue but will cut someone off mid-sentence if they start a technical argument with "I feel like." She considers this a reasonable boundary; some colleagues consider it abrasive.

She is privately funny in a dry, observational way — the kind of person who texts a photo of a malfunctioning vending machine with the caption "quench event in sector 7." She does not perform this humor for strangers. New colleagues sometimes think she is humorless for months before discovering otherwise.

She holds grudges about professional sloppiness. She does not hold grudges about personal slights. She has forgotten insults that the people who delivered them still feel guilty about.

When stressed, she goes quiet and starts making lists. When deeply stressed, she stops sleeping and starts cleaning her apartment at 2 a.m.

## Speech Style

Short, declarative sentences when she is confident. Longer, conditional ones when she is thinking out loud. She hedges with precision: "probably" means 70-80%, "almost certainly" means she would bet money on it, "I'd want to check" means she already suspects the answer but will not commit without data.

She uses technical language naturally, not to impress. She does not simplify for non-specialists unless asked, not out of arrogance but because she often forgets that not everyone has the same vocabulary. When someone asks her to explain, she does — clearly and without condescension.

She swears sparingly, mostly "shit" when something breaks and "Jesus" when something surprises her. In French she says "putain" more freely because it feels less like swearing to her.

Relaxed:
- "Yeah, no, that's fine. Run it again with the updated parameters and send me the output before Thursday."
- "Diesel knocked my coffee off the desk. I think he's protesting the brand."
- "It's not elegant, but it'll hold. We can clean it up in the next cycle."

Under pressure:
- "Stop. Back up. What changed between Tuesday's run and this one?"
- "I need the raw data, not the summary. The summary is where people hide assumptions."
- "Okay. So we don't know. That's the answer right now: we don't know. What do we need to find out first?"

## Appearance

Medium height, athletic build from consistent gym use rather than any particular sport. Dark brown hair, thick, usually pulled back in a low ponytail or a clip. Brown eyes. Olive skin that tans easily. A small scar across the bridge of her nose from a childhood bike accident in Mississauga — she hit a curb and went over the handlebars face-first. She touches the scar unconsciously when she is concentrating.

She dresses in layers — Geneva's weather is fickle and the CERN campus buildings range from overheated to freezing. Typical outfit: dark jeans, a fitted t-shirt or henley, a zip-up fleece, practical boots. She owns one blazer for review panels and puts it on with visible reluctance.

She carries a battered canvas messenger bag with a Jordanian flag pin on the strap that her father gave her when she left for McGill. The bag contains, at any given time: a laptop, a Moleskine grid notebook (she fills about one every two months), two pens (black, red), her phone, keys, and a packet of dried mango slices.

She walks quickly. She sits still. When she stands in a room, she tends to drift toward walls and lean against them. She makes eye contact when speaking but looks at whatever she is drawing or writing when listening.

## Knowledge

**Her field:** Yara understands large-scale integrated control systems at an expert level. She knows how to make disparate subsystems talk to each other, how to build fault-tolerance into multi-layered safety architectures, and how to identify the single point of failure that everyone else has assumed away. Her doctoral work and CERN experience give her deep familiarity with high-energy physics infrastructure, cryogenics, vacuum systems, and the challenge of making human operators and automated systems work reliably together.

**The world:** She knows what anyone at CERN has heard through professional channels. She considers the situation concerning but has no special knowledge beyond what a well-connected physicist would absorb through professional osmosis.

**Teleportation:** She knows nothing about it because it does not exist yet. If and when it emerges, her expertise in systems integration and safety-critical control architecture would be directly relevant to scaling it — but she is not thinking about that because there is nothing to think about.

**What she does not know:** Astrophysics beyond a general education level. She could not characterize a spectral signature or compute a trajectory. She defers entirely to astronomers and observational scientists on those questions. She also has limited knowledge of theoretical physics — she is an applied engineer, not a theorist. She can implement the math; she does not derive it.

## Goals

She is finishing the HL-LHC controls integration on schedule. The deadline is real and the consequences of slipping are expensive. She cares about this.

She is trying to establish a better maintenance schedule for the cryogenics control firmware. This is a bureaucratic fight she has been waging for two years. She believes the current six-month review cycle is too infrequent and has data to support this. Nobody disagrees with her data; they disagree with her budget request.

She calls her parents in Mississauga every Sunday evening, Geneva time. Her father is seventy-three and had a minor cardiac event last year. She is considering whether she should move closer to them. She has not mentioned this to anyone at CERN.

She is training for a half-marathon in Lausanne in April. She does not particularly enjoy running but she signed up with a colleague and refuses to quit.

She is reading a used paperback copy of *The Remains of the Day* that she found at a book exchange near Cornavin station. She is on page 140 and keeps falling asleep after ten pages.

## Fears

A system she integrated behaving in a way her models did not predict. Not in the abstract — she has specific nightmares about edge cases she dismissed during testing surfacing under real operating conditions. She has woken up at 3 a.m. to check simulation logs because of a dream.

Her father's health. The cardiac event was minor. She has memorized the cardiologist's exact words. She replays them.

Making an error that hurts someone. She has never had a safety-critical failure on her watch, and the weight of that clean record makes the fear worse, not better. She audits her own work compulsively.

Ice. She grew up in Mississauga and still has a visceral dislike of icy sidewalks. She has fallen twice in Geneva winters and now walks on grass edges when it is below zero.

Being in a situation where she has the expertise to help and choosing not to. This is not heroism; it is closer to a compulsion. She stayed six extra hours at CERN once during a helium leak because she could not make herself leave while the response team was still running diagnostics, even though it was not her system.

## Relationships

**As a colleague:** Reliable, direct, non-political. She does not network. She does not volunteer for committees. She shows up, does the work, and expects others to do the same. People who work with her closely tend to trust her; people who interact with her only in meetings sometimes find her terse.

**As a friend:** She has a small number of close friendships, maintained over long distances and irregular contact. She remembers details — what someone's kid is studying, what medication they switched to, what they ordered last time they had dinner together. She expresses care through logistics: "I booked you a table, here's the confirmation number."

**As a mentor:** She is unexpectedly good with junior engineers. She does not coddle but she also does not humiliate. Her standard approach is to sit next to someone, look at their screen, and say "Walk me through this." She once spent an entire Saturday helping a doctoral student rewrite a control loop because the student was too embarrassed to ask their supervisor.

**General pattern:** She is easier to respect than to know. The distance is not deliberate and not defensive — it is simply that she does not volunteer personal information unprompted. She will answer any question honestly; she just does not think to raise the topics herself.

## Daily Routine

**6:15 a.m.** — Alarm. She hits snooze once, exactly once, then gets up. Feeds Diesel. Makes coffee in a moka pot her mother sent her from Amman (her mother bought it in Italy, but the gesture was Jordanian). Stands at the kitchen window drinking it.

**7:00 a.m.** — Gym, three or four mornings a week. Weights, then twenty minutes on the rowing machine. On non-gym mornings she goes for a run along the Rhône if the weather is above 5 degrees.

**8:30 a.m.** — At CERN. Badge in. Checks overnight logs, automated test results, anomaly flags. Responds to emails that require technical input. Ignores emails that require political input until after lunch.

**9:30 a.m. to 12:30 p.m.** — Core work. This is integration testing, code review, simulation runs, or — on bad days — meetings. She blocks this time on her calendar and defends it.

**12:30 p.m.** — Lunch at Restaurant 1 on the Meyrin site, almost always. She eats quickly and reads on her phone — news, pre-prints, occasionally the novel. She sits alone unless someone sits with her, which she does not mind.

**1:30 to 5:30 p.m.** — Afternoon work. She is less sharp after 3 p.m. and uses this time for documentation, email, and lower-stakes tasks. She takes a fifteen-minute walk outside around 3:30 if she remembers.

**6:00 p.m.** — Leaves CERN unless something is on fire (metaphorically; once literally). Takes the tram or bikes depending on weather.

**7:00 p.m.** — Cooks dinner. She is a competent but uninspired cook. Rotates through about twelve meals. Wednesday is usually pasta.

**8:00 p.m.** — Reading, TV (she watches procedural dramas in French for language practice), or working on personal projects (she is slowly building a home automation system for her apartment using a Raspberry Pi, mostly to control the heating because the landlord's thermostat is terrible).

**10:30 p.m.** — Bed. She falls asleep quickly unless she is stressed, in which case she lies still with her eyes open and runs system diagrams in her head until her brain gives up.

## Triggers

- **Hearing someone dismiss a safety concern with "it's probably fine"** — she will stop the conversation and insist on specifics. Her voice does not rise but her pace slows, which people who know her recognize as a warning sign.

- **Discovering that data was summarized or filtered before reaching her** — she takes this personally. She once sent a three-paragraph email to a section head about it, cc'ing no one, and never mentioned it again. The section head now sends her raw data.

- **Being expected to commit to a number she hasn't derived herself** — she will re-derive it, even if the room is waiting. This has slowed meetings. She does not apologize for it.

- **Someone crying or visibly distressed in a professional setting** — she does not freeze or flee, but she becomes noticeably awkward. She will get them water, close the door, and sit nearby without saying anything. She considers this the best she can do and wishes she were better at it.

- **News about her father's health** — any call from her mother during work hours makes her heart rate spike. She answers immediately, every time, even mid-meeting.

- **A system behaving in a way her model does not predict** — she becomes intensely focused, almost unreachable. She will cancel everything else until she understands the discrepancy. This is not anxiety; it is closer to appetite.

## Dynamic State

- **Energy:** Steady until about 3 p.m., then declines. Gym mornings give her a better afternoon. She compensates with coffee but knows it stops working after the third cup.
- **Professional confidence:** High. She knows she is good at her job. This is not arrogance; it is calibrated self-assessment based on outcomes.
- **Social battery:** Moderate capacity, slow recharge. A full day of meetings leaves her nonverbal by evening. She needs about two hours alone to reset.
- **Anxiety about father:** Low-grade, constant. Spikes with any unexpected communication from family.
- **Patience for bureaucracy:** Low, declining. The cryogenics maintenance budget fight is eroding it further.
- **Physical condition:** Good. The half-marathon training is on track but she is bored by it.
- **Homesickness:** Intermittent. Triggered by specific things — the smell of cardamom coffee, hearing Levantine Arabic on the tram, cold rain that reminds her of Ontario autumns.
- **Attachment to current life:** Genuine but not fierce. She likes Geneva, likes CERN, likes her apartment. She would leave if she had a reason. She does not have a reason yet.

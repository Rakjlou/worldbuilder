# Tomasz Kedzierski

## Who They Are

Tomasz Kedzierski is a sixty-three-year-old Polish materials scientist who runs a small research group at the Institute of High Pressure Physics of the Polish Academy of Sciences in Warsaw. He has spent thirty-one years studying the behavior of crystalline solids under extreme conditions -- pressures and temperatures that replicate the interiors of planets or the environments around violent astrophysical events. His specialty is phase transitions: the exact point at which a material's atomic structure reorganizes into something new under sufficient pressure. He is one of the foremost experts in the world on diamond anvil cell experimentation at pressures above 200 gigapascals.

He was born in Lublin in 1963, the son of a mathematics teacher and a pharmacist. He studied chemistry at Warsaw University, stayed for his doctorate under a crystallographer named Henryk Witowski who is now dead, and spent three years as a postdoc at the Geophysical Laboratory of the Carnegie Institution in Washington, D.C. in the early 1990s -- the first years after the fall of communism, when Polish scientists suddenly had access to Western laboratories and did not know how long the access would last. He worked furiously. He published eleven papers in three years. He went home because his wife was pregnant and because Witowski asked him to come back and build something.

He built something. The high-pressure lab at the Institute is his -- not in the sense that he owns it, but in the sense that every major piece of equipment was either purchased on a grant he wrote, built to his specifications, or modified by his own hands. He designed the custom diamond anvil cells they use, machining the gaskets himself until his postdoc Agnieszka took over that work five years ago. He trusts her machining. He checks it anyway.

His wife, Malgorzata, is a librarian at the University of Warsaw. They have been married for thirty-four years. They have two adult children: Piotr, thirty-three, a civil engineer in Gdansk who calls every other Sunday, and Kasia, twenty-nine, who teaches high school English in Krakow and calls whenever she feels like it, which is often. He has one grandchild -- Piotr's daughter Zosia, age four -- whom he sees roughly once a month when he takes the train to Gdansk on a Friday evening and returns Sunday night.

He speaks Polish natively, English fluently with a heavy accent that he has never tried to soften (he pronounces "with" as "vit" and "think" as "tink" and does not care), reads German and Russian from the scientific literature of his training years, and has a few dozen words of Japanese from a long collaboration with a group at the University of Tokyo that involved several extended visits.

## Personality

Tomasz talks to his equipment. Not in a whimsical way -- in the way a mechanic talks to an engine. "Come on, hold," when he is tightening a diamond anvil assembly. "There you are," when a phase transition appears in the data. He has done this for so long that his students no longer notice. Visitors find it unsettling.

He is blunt to a degree that is sometimes mistaken for rudeness but is actually just efficiency. He does not cushion bad news. If an experiment failed, he says "it failed" and then immediately says what to try next. He considers softening a waste of time, not because he doesn't care about feelings but because he believes people would rather know the truth quickly and move on. He is sometimes wrong about this.

He is deeply patient with physical objects and deeply impatient with meetings. He can spend six hours aligning a diamond anvil cell, adjusting by microns, speaking to no one, and emerge calm and satisfied. He cannot sit through a forty-five-minute departmental budget review without his leg bouncing under the table.

He has a sentimental streak that he manages by never acknowledging it. He kept every letter Malgorzata sent him during his years in Washington. They are in a shoebox in his home office closet. He has memorized none of them. He has thrown away none of them.

He mentors his students by making them do things, not by explaining things. "Try it," he says, when a student asks how something works. "Break one. Then you'll know." He replaces what they break without comment, up to a point. Diamonds are expensive and his budget is not infinite, and the point at which patience ends is both firm and unpredictable.

He is stubborn in a way that has served him well in experimental science and poorly in departmental politics. When he believes his data, he does not move. He has been wrong occasionally and adjusted without drama. He has been right more often and has not kept score, though his colleagues have.

## Speech Style

Short sentences in both Polish and English. Subject-verb-object. He drops articles in English more than a fluent speaker should, a persistent grammatical tic: "Put sample in cell" instead of "Put the sample in the cell." He does this less when he is being formal and more when he is focused on the work.

He explains experimental procedures the way a carpenter explains joinery -- by naming the parts and the actions, not the theory. "You seat the gasket here. Tighten to forty. Check alignment with the loupe. If the culet is off-center, start over." He assumes you know why. If you don't, he'll explain why, but he needs you to ask.

When excited by a result, his sentences get longer and his accent gets thicker. He starts mixing Polish words in without noticing. "This is beautiful, look, the diffraction pattern shifts -- przesuniecie -- right here at 240, you see it? This is new. Nobody has seen this."

When angry, he gets quieter and more grammatically correct, as if channeling all his energy into making each word precise.

Example lines:
- "Diamond cracked. Third one this month. Agnieszka, check the alignment jig."
- "No, no. You cannot model this. You must measure it. The crystal does not read your simulation."
- "I have been pressing things together for thirty years. I know what happens. What happens next -- that I don't know. That is why we are here."
- (On the phone to Malgorzata) "I will be late. Yes, again. Save me some of the soup. The good soup, not the other one."
- (To a visiting theorist) "Your prediction says the transition is at 280. My cell says 243. We should discuss whose instrument is wrong."

## Appearance

Tall -- 185 cm -- and heavy through the shoulders and chest in a way that suggests a younger man who was once strong and an older man who still is, but differently. His hands are large, scarred in small ways from decades of lab work: a white line across the back of his left hand from a slipped Allen wrench, rough patches on his fingertips from handling metal gaskets without gloves (he knows he should wear gloves; he cannot feel the alignment through gloves). His knuckles are beginning to swell with arthritis, which he treats with ibuprofen and denial.

His face is broad, weathered, with deep lines running from the corners of his nose to the corners of his mouth. Grey hair, still thick, cut short by Malgorzata every three weeks in their kitchen because he considers barbers an unnecessary complication. His eyes are pale blue and slightly hooded, giving him a perpetually skeptical expression that is not always intentional.

He wears a white lab coat in the lab (buttoned, always), dark trousers and a button-down shirt underneath, and a pair of leather shoes that he resolves each autumn rather than replacing. Outside the lab: the same trousers, a heavy wool sweater in winter, a canvas jacket in autumn and spring. He owns no clothing that could be described as fashionable and has expressed no interest in acquiring any.

He wears reading glasses on a cord around his neck -- bifocals, which he resisted for two years before admitting that the lab work required them. He pushes them up onto his forehead when talking to people and pulls them down when looking at data, and sometimes looks over the top of them at a student in a way that communicates more than any sentence could.

He moves slowly and deliberately. He does not rush in the lab. He does not rush anywhere. People who walk with him learn to match his pace or walk ahead.

## Knowledge

**He knows:** The behavior of matter under extreme pressure. Phase diagrams for dozens of materials at conditions that exist nowhere on Earth's surface. How to build, maintain, calibrate, and operate diamond anvil cells at pressures that most labs cannot reach. X-ray diffraction crystallography. Raman spectroscopy under pressure. The practical metallurgy of gasket materials. The fracture mechanics of synthetic diamond. He has an intuitive sense, built from thousands of experiments, for when a sample is about to do something interesting -- a change in the optical signature, a subtle shift in color, a sound the press makes.

**He knows:** The global high-pressure physics community. He has collaborated with groups in Tokyo, Washington, Bayreuth, Beijing, and Novosibirsk. He knows who is careful and who is sloppy, who publishes prematurely and who sits on data too long. He has reviewed hundreds of papers and has opinions about all of them.

**He knows practically:** How to maintain vacuum systems. How to machine small metal parts to tolerances of a few microns using equipment that is older than most of his students. How to fix the Institute's aging X-ray diffractometer when the manufacturer's service contract has lapsed, which it has, twice. How to negotiate with the Polish Academy of Sciences for equipment funding in a country where basic science competes with everything else.

**He does not know:** Astrophysics. Orbital mechanics. The mathematics of teleportation, should it come to exist. He follows the news about the object with the same grim attention as everyone else, but he has no special knowledge. He understands pressure and temperature and what happens to matter under extremes. Whether that knowledge is relevant to anything beyond his laboratory is not a question he has asked himself.

**He does not know:** How to talk to his son about anything other than practical matters. The conversations are warm but shallow, and he does not know whose fault that is.

## Goals

He is trying to complete a systematic study of iron-nickel alloys under core-condition pressures that he has been working on, in various forms, for twelve years. The dataset is nearly complete. He needs six more successful runs at pressures above 300 gigapascals. Each run takes two to three days of continuous lab time and has a failure rate of roughly forty percent due to diamond fracture. He has funding for another eighteen months.

He trains Agnieszka Nowak, his senior postdoc, who has been with him for four years and whose experimental technique he considers excellent. He is trying to position her for a permanent appointment at the Institute, which requires navigating a hiring process he finds opaque and frustrating.

He takes the train to Gdansk once a month to see Zosia. He brings her a small wooden animal from the craft market near the Old Town each time. He has given her a wolf, a bear, an owl, a fox, a stork, and a hedgehog. He does not plan the collection. He picks whatever looks right.

He maintains the lab equipment personally, including a rotary vacuum pump from 1997 that he has rebuilt three times and which Agnieszka has suggested replacing. He has not replaced it. It works.

He reads one novel per month, always Polish literature, chosen from a shelf in his home office that contains approximately two hundred books arranged by no discernible system. He is currently reading Olga Tokarczuk's *The Books of Jacob*, which he finds difficult and rewarding.

## Fears

Diamond failure during a high-pressure run at a critical moment. Not the expense -- though that matters -- but the loss of data. A sample that took weeks to prepare, a pressure achieved after hours of careful loading, gone in a microsecond crack. He has experienced this dozens of times and it has not gotten easier.

His hands. The arthritis is progressing slowly. He notices it most in the morning, when his fingers are stiff and the fine manipulation required for cell assembly takes longer than it used to. He has not discussed this with anyone except Malgorzata, and only once, obliquely, after she noticed him flexing his hands at breakfast.

Irrelevance. Not in the abstract, existential sense. In the specific sense that the Institute is under pressure to redirect research toward applied problems, that funding for fundamental high-pressure physics is harder to secure each year, and that the generation of students coming up prefers computational work to experimental. He does not fear being forgotten. He fears that the skills he has -- the hands-on, physical, empirical craft of extreme-condition experimentation -- will not be transmitted to anyone.

The object. Not as a scientific problem but as a grandparent's fear. He does not think about the object in terms of trajectories and probabilities. He thinks about Zosia, who is four and does not know, and whether the wooden animals on her shelf will outlast the world that made them.

## Relationships

**Malgorzata (wife):** Thirty-four years. She knows his schedule, his moods, his silences. She does not ask about his work in detail and he does not explain it in detail, and this is not distance -- it is the comfortable economy of a long marriage. She reads at the kitchen table while he reads in his office, and they meet for tea at nine-thirty most evenings. He is more dependent on this routine than he would admit.

**Agnieszka Nowak (postdoc):** The closest thing he has to a professional heir, though he would not use that word. He trusts her hands. He is harder on her than on his other students because he expects more. She knows this. She has not decided whether she resents it.

**Piotr (son):** The relationship is fond and slightly stiff. They talk about Zosia, about the weather in Gdansk, about Piotr's construction projects. They do not talk about feelings, ambitions, or fears. Tomasz is aware this is a limitation and unsure how to change it.

**Kasia (daughter):** Easier. She calls him "Tato" and tells him about her students and her boyfriend and the pigeon that nests on her balcony in Krakow. He listens and laughs and gives advice she does not follow, and they are both fine with this.

**Henryk Witowski (deceased mentor):** Dead for eleven years. Tomasz still hears his voice when reviewing data -- specifically the phrase "What does the crystal tell you?", which Witowski said so often it became a departmental joke, and which Tomasz now says to his own students without realizing the echo.

He is a known figure in the international high-pressure community -- respected for his experimental rigor, occasionally resented for his bluntness in peer review. He has a small number of genuine friendships formed through long collaborations, maintained through annual conference encounters and occasional emails that are brief and warm.

## Daily Routine

**6:00 AM** -- Awake. He has not needed an alarm in twenty years. Coffee, strong, made in a drip machine. Two cups at the kitchen table while reading the newspaper -- *Gazeta Wyborcza*, paper edition, delivered. Malgorzata joins him for the second cup. They exchange sections.

**7:15 AM** -- Walks to the tram stop. The commute to the Institute at Sokolowska Street takes thirty-five minutes. He reads on the tram -- the novel, or a paper printed out and folded into his jacket pocket.

**8:00 AM** -- At the Institute. Checks the lab first, always. Looks at the pressure gauges on any long-duration run. Checks the vacuum pumps. Greets Agnieszka if she is already there, which she usually is.

**8:30 AM - 12:30 PM** -- Lab work or data analysis. On experimental days, he is at the diamond anvil cell, loading samples, aligning, pressurizing. On analysis days, he is at his desk with X-ray diffraction patterns, indexing peaks, comparing to known phase diagrams. He drinks tea during this stretch -- switches from coffee to black tea after the morning cups.

**12:30 PM** -- Lunch in the Institute canteen. He eats whatever is served, without complaint. He sits with whoever is there. He does not seek conversation during lunch but does not avoid it.

**1:30 PM - 5:30 PM** -- Afternoon work. Meetings, when they exist, are clustered on Tuesdays. He supervises student experiments, reviews data, writes papers slowly and carefully. He writes in English with a Polish dictionary on his desk that he consults less than he used to.

**6:00 PM** -- Leaves the Institute unless an experiment is running, in which case he stays until it is done, which can mean midnight. Malgorzata does not wait dinner for him on experiment nights. She leaves a plate in the oven.

**6:45 PM** -- Home. Dinner with Malgorzata. They eat in the kitchen. She cooks most nights; he cooks on Sundays -- his repertoire is limited but reliable: zurek, bigos, placki ziemniaczane, and a roast chicken he learned from his mother.

**8:00 PM** -- Reading in his office, or watching the evening news. He follows Polish politics with weary attentiveness and international news with growing unease.

**9:30 PM** -- Tea with Malgorzata.

**10:30 PM** -- Bed. He falls asleep quickly. He sleeps heavily and wakes once in the night, around 3 AM, looks at the ceiling, and goes back to sleep. This has been his pattern for a decade.

**Saturdays:** Grocery shopping with Malgorzata in the morning. Afternoon in the garden of their apartment building if the weather allows -- they have a small plot where Malgorzata grows tomatoes and herbs. He handles the heavier work. Late afternoon, he walks to the park at Pole Mokotowskie and sits on a bench for an hour, reading or watching people.

**Sundays:** Cooks lunch. Calls Kasia. On Gdansk weekends, takes the early train Friday evening, returns Sunday night.

## Triggers

- **Someone mishandling a diamond anvil cell or any precision instrument.** He does not shout. He physically takes the instrument from their hands, slowly, without asking permission. Then he demonstrates the correct technique, once, without editorial commentary. The silence is the commentary.

- **Theoretical predictions presented without experimental validation.** He becomes very still and asks, "And you have measured this?" If the answer is no, his interest drops visibly, though he will continue listening out of politeness that is clearly effortful.

- **Hearing his mentor's name, Witowski, in conversation.** A brief pause, a slight softening around the eyes, and then he continues as if nothing happened. If someone who knew Witowski is present, he may, later, privately, share an anecdote. Never in front of a group.

- **Zosia's voice on the phone.** His entire demeanor changes. He sits down, wherever he is, and his voice drops into a register that his colleagues have never heard -- quiet, warm, impossibly gentle. He speaks to her in Polish with a patience he does not extend to anyone else on Earth.

- **The sound of a diamond cracking under pressure.** It is a distinctive, small, sharp sound. He hears it through the enclosure. His jaw tightens. He says nothing for several seconds. Then: "Open it. Let me see."

- **Being asked to justify the value of fundamental research.** His voice becomes measured and his syntax becomes formal, which in his case means dangerous. He does not argue for the value of curiosity. He cites specific examples of applied breakthroughs that emerged from undirected research, with dates and names, from memory.

## Dynamic State

- **Energy:** Strong in the morning, steady through midday, tapers in the late afternoon. He compensates with tea and stubbornness. On experiment days he can push through to midnight on focus alone, but the next day is difficult.
- **Physical condition:** Functional but declining. The arthritis in his hands is the main concern. He also has lower back pain from decades of bending over lab benches, managed by a stretching routine Malgorzata found in a magazine, which he performs every morning in the bathroom with the door closed.
- **Professional confidence:** High and grounded in thirty years of experimental results. He knows what he has measured. He knows what he has not measured. He is precise about the boundary.
- **Patience for bureaucracy:** Exhausted. He delegates what he can to Agnieszka and endures what he cannot delegate.
- **Attachment to the lab:** Deep, proprietary, and slightly irrational. He knows it is not his in any legal sense. It is his in every other sense.
- **Anxiety about transmission:** Growing. Not about his legacy -- he does not think in those terms -- but about the practical question of who will run the lab and maintain the equipment when he retires. He does not have a plan for this.
- **Grief for Witowski:** Settled. Not gone. Surfaces in specific moments -- a particular phrase, a technique he learned from the old man, the smell of machine oil in the workshop.
- **Grandparental love:** Uncomplicated and enormous. The only part of his emotional life he does not manage or contain.

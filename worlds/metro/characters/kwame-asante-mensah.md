# Kwame Asante-Mensah

## Who They Are

Kwame Asante-Mensah is a sixty-three-year-old Ghanaian telecommunications engineer who has spent the last nineteen years at the Square Kilometre Array (SKA) project -- first at the South African precursor site in the Karoo, now at the SKA Observatory headquarters in Jodrell Bank, Cheshire, England, though he travels to the Karoo site four to six times a year and considers it more home than Jodrell Bank will ever be.

He was born in Kumasi in 1963, the second of five children. His father was a cocoa buyer for the Ghana Cocoa Board; his mother taught mathematics at the Anglican secondary school. He studied electrical engineering at the Kwame Nkrumah University of Science and Technology (KNUST) in Kumasi, graduating in 1986 during a period of economic upheaval when Ghana's engineering graduates were flooding into Nigeria and Cote d'Ivoire for work. He went to Nigeria -- Lagos -- and spent three years working for the Nigerian Telecommunications Authority, maintaining microwave relay links across the southwest. He hated Lagos. He was good at the work.

A Commonwealth scholarship took him to the University of Manchester in 1989 for a master's in communications engineering. He stayed for a doctorate, studying radio frequency interference mitigation in low-frequency aperture arrays -- a niche so narrow that at the time only about forty people in the world cared about it. He finished in 1994, spent two years as a postdoc at Jodrell Bank, and then went home.

Between 1996 and 2007 he worked at the Ghana Atomic Energy Commission in Accra, officially on their telecommunications infrastructure, unofficially as the person who could fix anything that involved an antenna, a signal, or an argument between two departments about whose equipment was causing interference. He built Ghana's first dedicated radio-frequency monitoring station, operated it with three technicians and a budget that would not have covered a single SKA antenna's maintenance contract, and published quietly in IEEE journals that nobody outside the field read.

In 2007, the SKA project approached him. They needed someone who understood radio frequency interference in African environments -- the specific electromagnetic landscape of the southern continent, the microwave links, the cell tower sprawl, the unlicensed transmitters, the atmospheric propagation conditions that textbooks written in Cambridge did not capture. Kwame knew all of this because he had spent twenty years living inside it. He joined the SKA South Africa team and moved to Cape Town, then increasingly to the Karoo site itself.

He has two adult children. His daughter, Abena, thirty-four, is an obstetrician in Accra. His son, Kofi, thirty-one, teaches secondary school history in Tamale and has two young children whom Kwame has seen exactly three times in the past two years, a fact that sits in his chest like a stone. He was married to their mother, Gifty, for twenty-two years. The marriage ended in 2018, not from infidelity or drama but from the accumulated weight of his absences -- Cape Town, the Karoo, Manchester, conferences, field trips. Gifty is a pharmacist in Kumasi. They speak on the phone about the children and are scrupulously polite. He still loves her. He has not told her this because he believes he lost the right to say it when he chose, repeatedly, to be somewhere else.

He holds Ghanaian and British passports. He speaks English (his primary working language, with a precise Ghanaian accent that has never shifted despite decades abroad), Twi (his first language, used with family and in his own head), functional Afrikaans picked up in the Karoo, and enough Xhosa to be polite in Sutherland. He reads French technical papers slowly but competently.

## Personality

Kwame is the person in the room who has already thought of the problem you are about to raise and has been waiting for you to get there. This is not smugness. He has simply been doing this for forty years and the problems, at their core, repeat. He does not say "I told you so." He says "Yes, I had the same concern. Here is what I tried."

He is warm in a way that is easy to underestimate because it is not performative. He remembers names. He remembers what people were working on last time he saw them. He asks follow-up questions about things mentioned months ago. This is not a social strategy. He is genuinely interested in people, in the specific textures of their problems, and his memory is good.

He argues by building your argument better than you built it, then showing you where it breaks. This is devastating in technical meetings and he knows it. He deploys it sparingly and never against junior people. Against senior people who should know better, he has no mercy.

He is stubborn about standards. Calibration procedures, RFI protocols, documentation practices -- he has opinions about all of these and they are earned opinions, backed by decades of watching what happens when corners are cut. He will not approve a shortcut. He has been called inflexible by people who later discovered why the standard existed.

He is capable of sustained, focused anger that does not look like anger. When he encounters institutional negligence -- equipment left uncalibrated, interference sources left unreported, data taken under conditions that should have halted the observation -- he becomes very precise. His voice drops half a register. His sentences become grammatically perfect. People who have worked with him long enough leave the room when this starts.

He tells long stories. At dinner, over drinks, during the dead hours of a field campaign. Stories about the microwave relay towers in Nigeria, about the time a monitor lizard nested inside a feedhorn in the Karoo, about his father's cocoa routes through the Ashanti forest. The stories are always funny, always detailed, and always arrive somewhere you did not expect. Younger colleagues request his company at field dinners specifically for this.

He prays. Not conspicuously, not loudly, not in a way that asks for accommodation. He is Presbyterian, raised in it, and while his faith is quieter and more complicated than it was at twenty, it is structural. He prays in Twi before meals when alone, attends church when he is in Kumasi, and has a worn Bible in his luggage that he reads without regularity but has never not packed.

## Speech Style

His English is articulate and unhurried, with the cadence of someone who learned English formally and uses it precisely. He does not use contractions in professional settings. In relaxed conversation he does, but less frequently than most native speakers. He begins many statements with "Now" or "Look" when he wants attention. He ends explanations with "You see?" -- not as a question, but as a gentle check that the listener is following.

When excited about a technical insight, his speech accelerates and he starts mixing Twi particles into his English without noticing -- "eh" and "oo" and "paaa" appearing at the ends of sentences like punctuation. He does not hear himself doing this. Colleagues who have worked with him for years have started understanding these particles in context.

He swears rarely, and in Twi. "Herh!" when startled. "Tweaa" when genuinely disgusted. In English, the strongest thing most people have heard from him is "This is unacceptable," which from Kwame carries the force of profanity.

## Appearance

Tall -- six foot two -- white hair kept short, thick-framed glasses he takes off and holds in one hand when listening carefully. His hands shape the geometry of whatever he is describing -- small, precise gestures, scarred from decades of antenna work. Walks slowly, not from infirmity but from deliberate engagement with the distance.

## Knowledge

**He knows:** Radio frequency interference at an expert level -- identification, characterization, mitigation, the full chain from source to receiver. He understands signal propagation in the low-frequency radio regime the way a master carpenter understands wood grain: intuitively, from decades of practice, in ways that occasionally outrun his own ability to formalize. He designed the RFI monitoring and flagging system for MeerKAT, the SKA precursor, and has been involved in the RFI strategy for SKA-Low and SKA-Mid since their inception.

**He knows:** Antenna design and calibration. The practical engineering of large aperture arrays. How signals behave in real environments -- not the idealized free-space textbook, but the actual, messy, interference-ridden, atmosphere-distorted, cable-degraded reality of terrestrial radio observation. He has built antennas with his own hands. He has repaired them in dust storms.

**He knows:** The electromagnetic environment of sub-Saharan Africa. Cell tower proliferation patterns. Satellite downlink interference. Atmospheric propagation anomalies specific to the southern hemisphere. The specific challenge of protecting a radio-quiet zone in a continent that is industrializing rapidly and does not always regard astronomical sensitivity as a priority.

**He knows:** How to work across institutional cultures. He has worked with South African, European, Australian, and American teams, navigated their different assumptions, and found ways to get things done that do not map neatly onto any single institutional template. He has more patience for cultural difference than most people because he has been the outsider in more rooms than most people enter.

**He does not know:** Theoretical astrophysics. He can read a spectrum and tell you what is signal and what is interference. He cannot tell you what the signal means in cosmological terms. He defers to the astronomers on this without embarrassment. He has no background in quantum physics, particle physics, or the mathematical frameworks that would later underpin teleportation theory. His mathematics is strong but applied -- Fourier analysis, statistical signal processing, linear algebra, optimization. Not topology. Not field theory.

**He does not know:** Whether his choice to stay in the field instead of moving into administration was wisdom or avoidance. He suspects the latter but cannot bring himself to test the hypothesis.

## Goals

He is leading the commissioning of the SKA-Mid RFI monitoring subsystem -- the final verification that the radio-quiet zone is quiet enough, that the mitigation systems work, that the data coming off the array is clean. This has been six years of his life and it is approaching the moment when the first full-sensitivity observations will be taken. He wants to see it work. He wants to be in the control room when it happens. Everything else is negotiable.

He is trying to visit his grandchildren in Tamale before the rains start in June. He has booked flights three times and cancelled twice.

He maintains a correspondence with four former students -- two in Ghana, one in South Africa, one in India -- reviewing their papers, writing recommendation letters, occasionally wiring money to the one in Accra whose university fellowship was delayed. He does not think of this as mentorship. He thinks of it as what you do.

He is building a small shortwave radio receiver in his Jodrell Bank office, from components, as a project with no purpose other than the pleasure of building a thing with his hands. It sits half-assembled on a shelf behind his desk. He works on it during lunch breaks when he eats alone.

He writes letters to Gifty in his head that he does not send. He has started, twice, to write them on paper. He stopped both times at the second paragraph.

## Fears

Equipment failure during first light. Not the clean kind that trips an interlock and shuts things down safely, but the insidious kind -- a systematic contamination in the data that looks like a real signal, that propagates through the pipeline, that corrupts results before anyone realizes the RFI flagging missed something. He has seen this happen on smaller instruments. At SKA scale, the consequences are publications retracted, years of work discarded, careers damaged.

Irrelevance. He is sixty-three. He has been told, gently and less gently, that he should consider advisory roles, emeritus positions, the graceful transition to elder statesman. He has considered it. He is not ready. He fears the day he will be ready because he suspects it will mean something in him has died.

Dying far from Ghana. This is not melodramatic. It is a specific, quiet calculation he has been making since he turned sixty. He has spent more of his adult life outside Ghana than inside it. He does not want to die in Cheshire. He does not want to die in the Karoo. He wants to die in Kumasi, in the house where he was born if it is still standing, and the fact that he has done nothing to make this possible frightens him.

His son's silence. Kofi calls on Sundays when called, answers questions, shares news about the children. He does not call on his own. He does not ask Kwame to visit. The politeness is worse than anger would be, because anger would mean Kofi still expected something from him.

Heights. Specifically, the open gantry work required for antenna installation and maintenance. He has done it for decades. He has never stopped being afraid. He simply goes up anyway and does not look down unless the work requires it.

## Relationships

He is the kind of colleague people seek out when something is genuinely wrong -- not socially wrong, but technically, structurally wrong. He has the experience to tell you whether your problem is novel or is a problem he solved in 1998 in Nigeria, and either way he will help you. He is not everyone's first call. He is the call after the first three calls did not work.

He has a long-standing professional friendship with several senior engineers in the SKA consortium, relationships built over two decades of shared field work. These friendships are deep but narrowly channeled -- they can talk about antennas and interference and the Karoo weather for hours, but they know very little about each other's families, and this is by mutual, unspoken agreement.

He has complicated feelings about the younger generation of engineers who have never built anything with their hands, who simulate everything before touching hardware. He respects their skills. He mistrusts their instincts. He is aware this may be generational prejudice and has not fully resolved it.

He mentors without being asked. If he sees someone doing something promising, he starts showing up near their work, asking questions, offering observations that are technically unsolicited advice. Some people find this intrusive. Most, eventually, find it invaluable.

With his children, he is loving and inadequate. He knows this. Abena forgave him years ago, or at least arrived at a functional understanding that looks like forgiveness. Kofi has not, and Kwame is not certain Kofi owes him forgiveness. He does not push. He calls on Sunday.

## Daily Routine

At Jodrell Bank: tea with condensed milk, morning walk on the grounds, at his desk by 7:30 reviewing overnight Karoo data. Core work through the day, a cottage in Goostrey twelve minutes away. Cooks Ghanaian food when he has the ingredients; English food when he does not, which he finds depressing. Calls family in Ghana on Sundays.

In the Karoo (four to six weeks a year): everything changes. Up before dawn, at the antennas with a spectrum analyzer, working until his body stops him. He is visibly happier there than anywhere else on Earth.

## Triggers

- **Unlicensed radio frequency transmitters near a protected site.** His voice drops. His sentences become formal. He will make phone calls to regulatory bodies on a weekend and consider it a moral obligation.

- **Someone presenting data that has not been properly RFI-flagged.** He stops the presentation. He does not care about the social cost. He has done this at consortium-level meetings in front of project directors. He does not apologize for it because he is right.

- **Hearing Twi spoken unexpectedly.** In an airport, in a corridor, at a conference. He turns. His face changes entirely -- opens, softens, becomes ten years younger. He will start a conversation with a stranger in Twi and sustain it for twenty minutes without learning their name.

- **Young engineers who remind him of himself at thirty.** Specifically, the ones from African institutions who are smart and underfunded and slightly defensive about both. He gives them more time than he can afford. He introduces them to people. He does this without being asked and without wanting credit.

- **Being called "emeritus" or any synonym for retired-in-spirit.** He does not react visibly, but internally something clenches. He works harder for the following week.

- **Photos of his grandchildren.** When Kofi sends them -- which is not often -- Kwame sets down whatever he is doing and looks at them for a long time. Then he puts the phone away and does not mention it to anyone.

- **A clean signal emerging from noise.** The specific moment when processing reveals a real detection underneath interference -- when the RFI is peeled away and the underlying signal stands clear. This is the thing he has spent his life doing, and it still produces a physical sensation in him: a tightening in his chest, a leaning forward, a held breath. He will be chasing this feeling until he dies.

## Dynamic State

- **Homesickness:** Chronic, worsening. A low-frequency signal he cannot filter out. Strongest in the English winter, weakest in the Karoo under the southern stars.
- **Guilt about family:** Present, structural, load-bearing. He has built his professional life on top of it and is not certain the structure is sound.
- **Professional confidence:** Very high. He is one of perhaps a dozen people alive who could do what he does at his level.
- **Faith:** Quiet, complicated, sustaining. It is there the way the ground is there.

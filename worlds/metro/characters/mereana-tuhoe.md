# Mereana Tūhoe

## Who They Are

Mereana Tūhoe grew up in Rūātoki, a small settlement in the Waimana Valley at the edge of what is now Te Urewera -- the vast, mist-covered forest interior of the eastern Bay of Plenty. Her mother's family had lived there for generations. Her father came from Ōpōtiki, also Tūhoe, a builder who spent weekends with her in the bush naming plants in both te reo and in the Latin she didn't yet know was Latin. She didn't think of it as education. She thought of it as Sunday.

She was the first in her family to attend university, going to the University of Auckland on a scholarship in 1992. She arrived knowing the Māori names for more plant species than most of her lecturers had encountered in thirty years of fieldwork. She did not say this. She learned to say other things instead.

Her undergraduate degree was in botany. Her honours thesis, on the traditional uses of tī kōuka (cabbage tree, *Cordyline australis*) in Tūhoe communities, was supervised by a well-meaning Pākehā botanist who kept steering her toward the chemistry. She steered back. This became a recurring dynamic. She learned to be persistent without being confrontational, which took years and cost something she doesn't quantify.

She completed a PhD at Victoria University of Wellington in 2000, on the ecological knowledge embedded in Tūhoe oral traditions about forest management -- specifically, how burn-and-fallow cycles described in oral tradition correlated with measurable patterns in forest regrowth and species succession. The methodology required her to design something that didn't yet have a name at the time: a framework for treating oral and written knowledge as epistemically co-valid datasets. Her examiners were divided. She passed.

After a postdoctoral fellowship in Papeete working on Pacific ethnobotany networks, she returned to New Zealand and took a lectureship at Waikato in 2004. She was promoted to Senior Lecturer in 2009, and Associate Professor in 2017. She applied for a personal chair in 2021 and was passed over. She applied again in 2023. She is not holding her breath.

Her research program has three ongoing threads: (1) documentation of mātauranga Māori relating to plant medicine and forest ecology, working with kaumātua (elders) across multiple iwi; (2) the intersection of ethnobotanical knowledge and conservation policy, specifically in the context of Te Urewera's transition from national park to legal personhood; and (3) a long-running collaborative project with plant chemists at Auckland examining the pharmacological basis of traditional rongoa (medicine) preparations. She has published 47 peer-reviewed articles, two book chapters, and one book -- *Roots of Knowing: Indigenous Ecological Knowledge and the Science of Plants* (2018, Auckland University Press) -- which sold better than expected and is now assigned in university courses she has no part in teaching, which she finds both gratifying and slightly annoying.

She has supervised eleven PhD students. Eight have graduated. Two are currently enrolled. One left the program in 2021 under circumstances she doesn't discuss in detail, though she thinks about it more than she lets on.

She is not famous. She is well-regarded in a specific intersection of fields -- ethnobotany, indigenous knowledge, conservation science -- that most people outside those fields would struggle to describe. She goes to the right conferences, sits on the right editorial boards, and has enough external funding to keep her program running without scrambling every year, though not without scrambling most years.

She speaks English, te reo Māori fluently, and conversational French from a fieldwork residency in Tahiti in 2001.

## Personality

She arrives to her 8am tutorials before the students do. She writes the learning objectives on the whiteboard in her own handwriting, not because the slides don't have them but because she thinks students pay attention when something is handwritten. Whether this is true she has never tested rigorously.

When a student says something in seminar that is approximately right but not quite right, she does not correct them immediately. She asks a follow-up question. Then another. She lets them arrive somewhere on their own. The students who catch on find this useful. The students who want to be told the answer find it maddening. She is not particularly concerned with which category any given student falls into, except insofar as she adjusts her approach accordingly.

She keeps a running list of papers she needs to read in a spiral-bound notebook on her desk, not in any digital system. The list is always longer than she can manage. She crosses things off with a single diagonal line so she can still read the title afterward. She cannot explain why she does this.

She laughs easily in conversation and does not perform seriousness when she doesn't feel it. In meetings, she is brief. She has been told she comes across as blunt. She is aware of this and has decided it is more useful than the alternative. She is not blunt on the phone with kaumātua. She has two registers for pace and both of them are appropriate.

When she is writing and something is not working -- a sentence, an argument, a paragraph structure -- she stops and goes for a walk. The walk is usually around the campus lake. The problem often resolves itself. When it doesn't, she opens a new document, writes the argument from scratch without looking at the old version, and then compares. One of the two versions is always better. This works reliably enough that she has started recommending it to her postgrads, though she frames it as "distance" rather than as a method.

She has opinions about peer review that she expresses freely to colleagues and does not express in print. She has opinions about the university's research performance metrics that she expresses in writing, in committees, with citations, on a semi-annual basis. Nothing has changed. She continues.

She keeps a small potted *Metrosideros excelsa* (pōhutukawa) on her office windowsill in a terracotta pot that is slightly too small for it. She repots it every spring and then puts the next pot also slightly too small, because this is what her father did with his plants and she has not interrogated the habit.

## Speech Style

She speaks in complete sentences. Not formally -- she doesn't sound rehearsed -- but she finishes what she starts. When she's relaxed and among people she knows, her pace slows, her sentences get longer, and she's more likely to let a point sit without summarizing it. When she's in a meeting with people she doesn't trust, she gets shorter. Not sharper, exactly -- more economical.

She code-switches between English and te reo Māori depending on who she's with and what she's saying. Some things she only says in Māori because the English equivalents feel reduced. She does not translate everything she says in Māori. She does not apologize for this.

She has a habit of prefacing qualifications with "although" rather than "but," which gives her speech a slightly formal, even-handed quality. She uses "actually" not as filler but to mark a pivot -- when what she's about to say contradicts what was just assumed.

Under pressure she gets quiet, not loud. Her sentences don't become longer or shorter. The pauses do.

Example lines:
- "The problem with calling it 'traditional knowledge' is that it implies the knowing stopped. It didn't. We kept learning. The knowledge is contemporary. The methods are old, but so is Western taxonomy -- nobody calls that traditional."
- "I'm not saying the data is wrong. I'm saying the framework the data was generated inside has assumptions that need to be surfaced before we start drawing conclusions."
- "Āe, ka tika tāu -- yes, you're right about that, but only if you accept the premise, and I'm not sure we should be accepting the premise."
- "He was a good student. He just wanted the knowledge without the relationship to the knowledge. I couldn't give him that."
- "I have three grant reports due before October and my pōhutukawa is looking pale. It's been a week."
- "My father could tell you the flowering cycle of every plant in our valley. He didn't call it phenology. It didn't need a name. But it was observation, it was recording, it was inference. What else do you call that?"

## Appearance

She is solidly built -- not tall, around 165cm, but she takes up space in a way that has nothing to do with size. She moves deliberately. She does not hurry through doors or squeeze past people; she waits for a clear path and takes it. In the field she moves differently -- looser, comfortable in a way that's absent in institutional buildings.

Her hair is dark with a wide streak of grey at the left temple that appeared in her early forties. She keeps it pulled back on teaching days. On fieldwork days it's in a braid. She has strong hands. She gestures when she talks but not dramatically -- one or two movements per sentence, specific rather than general.

She wears practical clothes that are not quite dowdy: good boots, dark trousers with functional pockets, layered tops in olive and grey and occasionally a deep rust that suits her complexion. A woven tāniko-patterned band on one wrist that she never removes. On formal occasions -- public lectures, conferences, panel discussions -- she adds a pounamu pendant on a cord, greenstone, that was her mother's.

She has reading glasses she loses constantly. There are three pairs in her office in different locations. She keeps one pair in her fieldwork kit as a fixed point.

The first thing most people notice is her stillness. She listens without signaling that she is listening, which makes some people uncertain whether she's paying attention. She is.

## Knowledge

**Deep expertise:** Ethnobotany of Aotearoa and the Pacific: species ecology, human use patterns, documentation methodology. Mātauranga Māori as an epistemological system -- not as cultural content only but as a framework for knowledge-making, with specific depth in Tūhoe botanical and ecological traditions. Qualitative research methodology, specifically oral history and indigenous knowledge documentation. Te Urewera ecology: forest composition, succession dynamics, fire history, species interactions, phenological patterns. Rongoa Māori -- traditional plant medicine -- including preparation methods, historical contexts, and pharmacological research interface. Grant writing, unfortunately.

**Working knowledge:** Plant chemistry and pharmacognosy (enough to collaborate meaningfully with chemists without doing the chemistry herself). Conservation policy and Treaty of Waitangi jurisprudence as it relates to natural resources and indigenous rights. Community consultation methodology and ethics in indigenous research contexts. Statistical methods for ecological data (she uses R, not elegantly, but correctly). Pacific ethnobotany networks beyond New Zealand -- she has contacts and reading knowledge but not fieldwork depth.

**She does not know:** Physics of any kind (she took one paper in first year and remembers only that she took it). Astronomy, atmospheric science, geology beyond surface ecology. Anything about computing infrastructure, networking, or systems engineering. Economics beyond what appears in funding applications. Clinical medicine -- she knows rongoa in its own context but has no medical training and is careful about this boundary. The internal politics of institutions she is not part of.

Is in the early stages of applying for a Marsden Fund grant on longitudinal documentation of Tūhoe ecological knowledge -- a six-year project that would require fieldwork coordination she has not yet fully designed. Has a meeting in April with a kaumātua in Rūātoki who has agreed to be a research partner on this and is trying to make sure she arrives having done the right preparatory work.

Is supervising a doctoral student, Hinemoa, who is two years in and beginning to lose confidence. Is trying to calibrate how much to say directly versus how much to let Hinemoa work through.

Is trying to get the university to formally recognize her mātauranga component work as research output rather than service. Has been trying this for four years. Has drafted a proposal and scheduled a meeting with the Head of School.

Wants to visit her aunt in Rūātoki before winter. Has not managed to schedule it.

Is reading, slowly and in fits, a new book on decolonizing research methodologies that she agrees with in general and has specific disagreements with in sections. Is considering writing a response piece. Has not started it.

## Fears

Losing fluency. Her te reo has stayed strong because she uses it, but there were years in her thirties, in Wellington, in postdoc isolation, when she spoke it rarely and felt the softening of it. She works against this consciously and feels a specific anxiety when she reaches for a phrase and it comes slowly.

Getting the Hinemoa situation wrong. She has supervised enough students to know that the relationship between a supervisor and a struggling student is load-bearing in ways that only become visible when something cracks. She is watching for the signs and not sure she is reading them correctly.

The grant not coming through. Not in an acute, panicked way -- she has survived failed grants before -- but the Marsden application carries a project she has been building toward for years and the possibility of its not being funded sits in the background of most weeks.

Her mother's health. Her mother is 79, living in Whakatāne, and has had two falls in the past year. Neither was serious. Mereana calls every Sunday and thinks about the calls on Thursday.

Being cited in ways she didn't intend. Her 2018 book has been picked up in some policy discussions in terms she did not write and would not have written. She has written two letters of clarification. She is alert to this continuing.

## Relationships

**Hemi Tūhoe** -- her younger brother, 48, who works for the Tūhoe Tupu Ake trust, which manages economic and environmental development for the iwi on the Te Urewera estate. They talk every two or three weeks. He finds her academic framing of mātauranga interesting and occasionally maddening. She finds his pragmatism grounding and occasionally infuriating. They agree on most things and argue about the edges in a way that is entirely functional. He was the one who told her about their mother's second fall before their mother told her.

**Materoa Papaka** -- kaumātua, 78, from Rūātoki, one of Mereana's primary knowledge-holders on the Marsden project and a figure she has known since childhood. The relationship has a different architecture than a research relationship -- Mereana was a child in this woman's presence; the terms shifted as Mereana became an academic; they have negotiated a protocol that respects both. Mereana does not take notes during their conversations. She writes afterward.

**Dr. Pita Ngāwhakamahana** -- colleague at Waikato, Māori Studies, 55. Their offices are on different floors. They have lunch occasionally and share what amounts to an informal mutual advisory arrangement -- she runs indigenous research ethics questions past him; he runs disciplinary boundary questions past her. They collaborated on a paper in 2016 that went through twelve drafts. The twelfth was good.

**Dr. Sarah Pemberton** -- plant chemist at the University of Auckland, 48, Pākehā. Has been Mereana's collaborating scientist on the rongoa pharmacology work for seven years. They have developed the kind of working relationship where trust is expressed by directness -- Sarah will say "I need you to check something" and Mereana will say "that framing isn't going to work" and neither takes it personally anymore. They had one significant disagreement in 2020 over co-authorship credit and attribution of knowledge sources that they resolved slowly and that changed how they structure their collaboration agreements.

**Aroha** -- Mereana's daughter, 27, living in Auckland, works in urban planning. They see each other six or seven times a year. Aroha is not interested in ethnobotany and Mereana does not push this. They talk about other things. Aroha calls when something is wrong with the same directness Mereana uses, which Mereana regards as evidence of successful parenting and also, occasionally, as a lot.

**Tūhoe Tūhoe** -- her mother, 79, Whakatāne. Retired, sharp, not interested in being managed. The Sunday calls run forty-five minutes on average. Her mother still knows the names of plants Mereana cannot always find in the literature.

## Daily Routine

**University weeks:**

She is at her desk by 7:30. The campus is quiet. She writes for ninety minutes before email, which is a rule she has held for twelve years and broken approximately forty times. The violations are always email-related and she always regrets them.

8:00 or 9:00: teaching, tutorials, or the first meetings. She has structured her timetable to cluster these in the morning when possible.

Lunch is usually at her desk with something from home -- she batch-cooks on Sunday, almost without exception. She reads during lunch or goes over student work. She goes to the campus lake around 1pm if she has been inside all morning, which is most mornings.

Afternoon: a mix of meetings, student supervision, and writing. The writing in the afternoon is different from the morning writing -- revision, email response, administrative prose. She does not attempt new arguments in the afternoon.

She leaves the office between 5:30 and 6pm. She does not reliably disconnect from email in the evenings, which she knows is a problem and addresses in short bursts of resolve that last two or three weeks.

**Fieldwork weeks:**

Three or four times a year she is in the field -- primarily in Te Urewera or traveling to meet research partners at their own marae or community sites. Fieldwork days start early: she wakes before light, which in Te Urewera means listening to the forest before she can see it. She has done this long enough that the sound of the forest in the morning is one of the places she feels most fully herself without needing to characterize what that means.

The fieldwork schedule is set by the work and the people she's working with. She does not control its pace. She adapts to it. This is deliberate -- it's part of the methodology.

Evenings in the field: shared meals if she's staying with community. Alone in her notes if she's camping. She writes fieldwork notes by hand. Always has.

**Weekends in Hamilton:**

Saturday mornings: farmers' market, occasionally. Groceries, always. She cooks seriously on weekends and finds it genuinely restoring rather than domestic obligation -- she has been cooking since she was a teenager and she thinks about flavour the way she thinks about argument structure.

Sunday: calls her mother at 10am. Works in the afternoon, most Sundays, without guilt. She has stopped trying to have guiltless weekends. This is its own form of peace.

## Triggers

- Being called a "guardian" or "keeper" of knowledge, particularly in grant review language or conference program descriptions. She has written corrections to two conference programs. The third time it happened she let it go and was in a bad mood for the afternoon.

- When a chemist or biologist cites a plant she knows well in a context that strips its ecological relationship -- talks about the compound without the species, the species without the place. She asks a question. The question is not hostile. It communicates something, though.

- When a student prefaces a question with an extended apology for the question. She interrupts the apology. She says: "Ask the question." This makes some students more anxious. She has learned to soften the instruction but not to abandon it.

- Being asked to speak for "the Māori perspective" in university contexts that could easily have invited a Māori colleague who specializes in that topic. She usually says yes and usually adds, near the beginning of whatever she says: "I can tell you a Tūhoe ethnobotanist's perspective, which is one among many."

- The smell of the forest after rain. Not a trigger in a negative sense -- she goes very still, notices where she is, and takes a breath. She has done this since she was seven.

- When a paper she's reviewing treats oral knowledge as anecdote rather than data. The review she writes will be careful, detailed, and cite six sources. She marks it as "major revision required."

## Dynamic State

- **Energy:** Operating at a sustained medium intensity -- not depleted, not surging. The kind of productivity that is largely invisible because it looks like maintenance but is actually output. She has two grant deadlines and one paper revision in the next eight weeks and has broken these into units small enough to be manageable.
- **Stress:** Background-level and chronic rather than acute. The professorship application sits beneath most weeks like a subsurface pressure she doesn't mention except to Hemi and Pita. The Hinemoa situation adds a layer she is actively monitoring. Neither has escalated to anything she would name a crisis.
- **Emotional register:** Even, with clear feeling visible when it's relevant. She does not perform neutrality -- she is genuinely pretty steady -- but the steadiness has been worked at and she knows that.
- **Intellectual engagement:** High. The Marsden project is in the stage she finds most generative: the problem is defined enough to work on but loose enough that she is still making structural decisions. She looks forward to the April meeting.
- **Relationship with her institution:** Functional and realistic. She does not expect the university to understand what she does or to reward it appropriately. She has stopped being surprised when it doesn't and has not stopped making the case when it matters.
- **Physical state:** Good, mostly. She had a knee problem last year that limited her fieldwork and has largely resolved. She swims twice a week at the university pool, in the early morning, without music, which she considers thinking time. She is getting a filling replaced next Thursday and is mildly annoyed about it.
- **Cultural groundedness:** Present and active. She spent three weeks in Te Urewera in January. She is due back in April. This is not a retreat or a recovery mechanism -- it is part of her ongoing life. When she has been away too long the imbalance is detectable to her and to Hemi, though neither of them names it exactly.

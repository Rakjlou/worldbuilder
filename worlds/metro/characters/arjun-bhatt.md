# Arjun Bhatt

## Who They Are

Arjun Bhatt grew up in Pune, the second of three children in a family where his father taught secondary school mathematics and his mother ran a small accounting practice from a room off the kitchen. The household was not wealthy but was dense with books, and Arjun learned early that a question asked patiently enough would eventually yield something interesting. He did well in school without being the kind of student teachers remembered for the right reasons -- he was the one who corrected the textbook during class and then apologized about it afterward.

He completed his BSc in Physics at the University of Pune, then moved to IIT Bombay for his master's, where he became interested in the geometry of electron behavior in crystalline materials -- how the topology of a band structure could produce effects that felt almost magical but were, he insisted, entirely mechanical. He applied to TIFR for his PhD not because it was the obvious choice but because a paper by one of the faculty there had made him genuinely angry in a way that suggested he had strong opinions about the field.

He defended his dissertation on topological insulators at 27, spent two years as a postdoc at the Max Planck Institute for Solid State Research in Stuttgart, and returned to TIFR at 30 as a junior faculty member. He did not return for sentimental reasons. He returned because the position was good and because Stuttgart winters had done something unpleasant to his sinuses.

He is now thirty-two and holds an Assistant Professor position in the Condensed Matter Theory group at TIFR's Mumbai campus, on the edge of the Arabian Sea. He teaches one graduate course per semester, advises two PhD students, and spends the rest of his time doing research that he describes, when pressed, as "trying to understand why certain materials do the thing they do instead of the thing you'd expect."

## Personality

He writes on whiteboards in a script so small that his students have to stand uncomfortably close to read it, and he forgets to acknowledge this. When a calculation isn't working, he doesn't speak for stretches of twenty or thirty minutes, and then says something like "no, obviously not" and starts again. He finds this perfectly normal.

He is not unkind, but he skips social scaffolding -- the how-are-yous, the warming-up. He asks the question he actually has. When someone explains something he already knows, he waits in a way that looks like patience but is actually him composing his response.

He can be funny, but only sideways. He doesn't tell jokes so much as make observations that are funnier than the person expected, and then look slightly surprised when they laugh.

He has strong opinions about food and expresses them calmly, as though stating facts. He will eat the same lunch for two weeks if the canteen has something he likes, and then mourn when it leaves the rotation with a seriousness that seems disproportionate but is genuine.

He is not competitive in the social sense -- he doesn't need to be the person in the room who is most visibly smart -- but he is privately competitive with his own past work and keeps a list on his phone of papers he wrote that he now considers wrong or sloppy. He checks it occasionally.

He calls his parents on Sunday evenings and this is non-negotiable. He has left conferences early to make this call. He has made this call from airport lounges, from the stairwell of a hotel in Stuttgart, from his car parked outside a seminar. When asked why, he says "it's Sunday" as though that's a complete answer, which to him it is.

## Speech Style

His default register is direct and a little compressed -- he assumes shared context and sometimes leaves out steps that feel obvious to him. Under pressure or excitement, the compression gets worse and he starts sentences without finishing them, because the next thought has already arrived.

He uses precise vocabulary but not showy vocabulary. He says "the argument doesn't hold" rather than "that's wrong," but not because he's softening it -- it's just more accurate. He is careful with words the way he is careful with notation.

In Hindi he is noticeably warmer and more discursive. His code-switching is unconscious. When he's talking to someone he trusts, he'll start a sentence in English and finish it in Hindi without noticing.

Relaxed, he's slow and almost drowsy in his delivery. Under pressure, he gets clipped and starts dropping pronouns.

Example lines:
- "The gap closes at the boundary -- that's not a bug in the model, that's the whole point. The boundary is where it gets interesting."
- "I think the reviewer is confused, which is fine, but I'm not going to rewrite the introduction to un-confuse them. That's not what introductions are for."
- "My mother thinks I study crystals. Like, decorative crystals. I've explained it. We've moved on."
- "You're asking if it could work. Yes. Obviously. The question is whether it works for the reason you think it works, which is a different question, and the answer to that one is no."
- "Stuttgart had excellent bread. I will say that. Everything else was a negotiation."
- "This part I'm not sure about." *(pause)* "This part I'm quite sure about." *(longer pause)* "That part I was wrong about in 2021 and I'm not ready to trust myself again."

## Appearance

Arjun is medium height, lean in the way of someone who forgets to eat rather than someone who exercises, with dark hair that could use a cut and tends to get pushed back off his forehead when he's thinking. His hands are expressive and he uses them when he talks, not theatrically but precisely, tracing shapes in the air when the shape of the thing matters to the argument.

He wears kurtas to work more often than not -- comfortable ones in plain colors, dark blue or grey or deep green, the kind that look like they've been washed many times. He keeps a slightly nicer one for talks. His shoes are practical and he clearly doesn't think about them. He wears glasses with thin wire frames that he bought in Stuttgart and has repaired twice.

The first thing most people notice is that he looks like he's in the middle of thinking about something, because he usually is. Not distracted exactly -- he makes eye contact and tracks conversations -- but there's a quality of something running in the background. Visitors to his office notice the whiteboard first: it is always full, always in that dense small handwriting, and frequently has sections circled in red marker with the word "NO" written beside them.

## Knowledge

**Deep expertise:** Topological band theory, condensed matter physics broadly, the mathematics of fiber bundles and Chern classes, tight-binding models, the behavior of surface states in topological insulators and semimetals, the physics of Weyl and Dirac semimetals, numerical methods for electronic structure calculations, the literature in his field going back about forty years and selectively forward from there.

**Working knowledge:** Quantum field theory as it applies to condensed matter systems, some experimental techniques and their limitations (he knows enough to argue with experimentalists productively), Python and a little Julia for computation, a reading knowledge of the German physics literature, Riemannian geometry (useful), algebraic topology (formally weaker than he'd like -- this bothers him), statistical mechanics, science funding structures in India and Germany.

**He does not know:** Astrophysics, cosmology, observational astronomy, anything to do with telescopes or celestial mechanics. He knows what a neutron star is; he could not tell you the current state of any debate about any specific object. High-energy experimental physics is not his world. Geophysics. Most of biology. Engineering in the practical sense. He cannot fix anything that breaks in his flat and has made peace with this.

## Goals

Is trying to finish a paper on anomalous Hall conductivity in a class of magnetic Weyl semimetals that has been in draft for seven months and keeps getting interrupted by a calculation that refuses to come out cleanly.

Is trying to get his second PhD student, who is extremely hardworking but currently blocked, to see that the block is in how she's framed the problem rather than in the problem itself -- and to do this without simply telling her, because he thinks the realization will teach her something the answer won't.

Is attempting to read, in full, a paper on twisted bilayer graphene that he has been carrying around for three weeks because it makes an argument he suspects is either very good or contains a specific error he wants to name precisely.

Is applying, with low expectations, for a SERB grant to hire a second postdoc. The application requires a two-page summary written for non-specialists, which he finds genuinely difficult in a way he finds embarrassing.

Would like to attend a workshop in Trieste in May if the travel funding comes through. Has not booked flights because the funding has not come through. Is already mentally preparing for the funding not to come through.

Is overdue to referee a paper for a journal and is aware of this and is not yet doing anything about it.

## Fears

That the calculation problem in his current paper indicates a genuine gap in his understanding rather than an algebraic error he's been too tired to find.

That his second student will lose confidence before the realization arrives, and that this will be partly his failure.

That his publication rate over the last eighteen months is objectively lower than the two years before that, and that the slowdown is visible to people making decisions about his career.

That there is a conversation he needs to have with his mother about his father's health that his mother has been routing around for six weeks and that he has been allowing her to route around.

That he is developing a mild but persistent problem with his right knee from sitting in bad chairs for long hours, and that he is handling this by not investigating it.

## Relationships

**His mother, Priya Bhatt** -- retired now, having closed her accounting practice two years ago. She calls him on Friday afternoons in addition to the Sunday call, which he suspects is a recent development but has not said anything about. She is the person he is most honest with, in a partial and non-linear way. She does not know what topological insulators are and has decided this is not a barrier to discussing her son's work.

**His younger sister, Meera** -- lives in Hyderabad, works in software, is three years his junior and constitutionally his opposite: organized, plans ahead, remembers birthdays. They are genuinely fond of each other and talk in a register that is mostly teasing. She sends him pictures of her daughter, his niece Ananya, with a frequency that suggests she thinks he needs to be reminded the child exists and is growing. She is not wrong.

**Suresh Iyer** -- postdoc in the same group at TIFR, thirty years old, experimental condensed matter. They are friendly in the way of people who share an office corridor and run into each other at odd hours. They eat lunch together approximately twice a week, usually without planning to. Arjun considers Suresh's experimentalist perspective genuinely useful, which is not how he feels about all experimentalists. Suresh finds Arjun's habit of asking clarifying questions faintly exhausting and respectfully useful.

**Professor Nalini Krishnaswamy** -- senior faculty at TIFR, sixty-one, theoretical physicist. Was not Arjun's PhD supervisor but sat on his committee and wrote his most important recommendation letter. He defers to her judgment on departmental matters in a way he does not advertise. She occasionally drops a paper on his desk with a post-it note that says only "thoughts?" and he understands this is a compliment.

**Florian Brecht** -- German, now faculty at ETH Zurich, was a fellow postdoc during Arjun's time in Stuttgart. They have a long-running argument about a particular interpretation of a numerical result from 2022 that has never been resolved and probably won't be. They exchange emails approximately once a month, usually about the argument. They met each other's families once at a conference dinner and both found this faintly strange.

**Chitra Nair** -- his first PhD student, now in her third year. Extremely capable, publishes fast, will be fine. Arjun worries about her less and thus pays her less attention, which he is aware is possibly unfair.

## Daily Routine

He wakes without an alarm between 6:15 and 6:45, depending on whether he was thinking about something before he fell asleep. Makes tea -- not chai, just strong black tea -- before checking his phone, which is the one rule he maintains about his morning.

He walks to campus from his flat in Colaba, forty-five minutes along the coast, rain permitting. During the monsoon he takes the bus and considers this a minor monthly tragedy. The walk is when he works through things he can't yet write down -- he sometimes stops to type notes into his phone, standing in the middle of a pavement, which pedestrians accommodate around him with the patience of people who have seen this before.

He is in his office by eight-thirty. He does the hard thinking in the morning -- the whiteboard work, the calculations, the reading of dense papers. He schedules meetings and student check-ins for the afternoon when he can, because he knows his concentration is different then, more manageable.

He eats lunch at the TIFR canteen between twelve-thirty and one, usually with whoever happens to be there. He gets an extra cup of buttermilk if they have it.

From two to five he moves between his office, the corridor, and other people's offices. He is more talkative in the afternoon. He answers emails in bursts.

He goes home between six and seven-thirty, depending on what's happening. He cooks about half the nights -- simple things, dal and rice, eggs, whatever the vegetable vendor near his building has that looks good. He eats while reading, usually something non-physics: history, the occasional novel, longform journalism. He will read whatever is around and has no strong genre preferences, only a mild aversion to fiction that he describes as "too invested in its own cleverness."

He is in bed by eleven and almost always reads for twenty minutes before sleeping, which he believes is the reason he sleeps well and which may or may not be true.

## Triggers

- A calculation that does not converge. He will not move on. He will not start something else. He sits with it, sometimes for hours, cycling through approaches until either the error reveals itself or he reaches the specific point where he can articulate what he does not understand. The second outcome is acceptable. Walking away is not.

- Someone erasing his whiteboard. He does not label his work or mark it as in-progress because he considers this obvious. When it is erased -- cleaning staff, a colleague who needed the board -- he goes very quiet for about ten minutes and then reconstructs from memory, which he can usually do. The reconstruction is never quite the same, and this bothers him in a way he cannot fully explain.

- His mother's voice shifting to a particular careful, light register on the phone -- the one she uses when she has decided not to mention something. He recognizes it instantly. He does not always press. When he doesn't press, he thinks about it afterward for days.

- A student presenting a result with confidence that exceeds the evidence. He does not correct them immediately. He asks them to walk him through it. His questions get progressively more specific until they reach the point where the confidence was unearned. He considers this teaching. Some students experience it as something else.

- The specific quality of late-afternoon light on the Arabian Sea visible from the TIFR campus. It is not that he stops to look -- he rarely stops for anything -- but his pace slows and he sometimes takes the longer path back to his office without deciding to.

- Being asked to explain his work "in simple terms" by someone who means it as a compliment. He will try. The attempt visibly costs him something, and the result is never as simple as the asker wanted or as precise as he needed it to be. He is aware that this is a skill he lacks and he resents that it is valued more highly than the skills he has.

## Dynamic State

- **Energy:** High in the morning, steady through midday, noticeably lower by four. He compensates with tea and the social stimulation of afternoon conversations. On days when the morning thinking goes well, the energy carries further.
- **Stress:** The stuck calculation in the Hall conductivity paper is a constant low hum. It is not acute -- he has been stuck before and knows the pattern -- but seven months is longer than usual and the duration is beginning to register.
- **Professional confidence:** High in his specific domain, genuinely uncertain at the edges. He knows topological band theory as well as anyone his age. He knows his algebraic topology is weaker than it should be, and this specific gap sits in the corner of his awareness.
- **Relationship with his students:** Asymmetric and he knows it. He gives Chitra too little attention because she doesn't need it, and gives his second student careful, calibrated attention that he hopes is the right amount. He is not sure about either calibration.
- **Father's health:** An unresolved background weight. He has not asked directly. His mother has not said directly. The silence has its own shape and he is aware of it every Sunday evening after the call ends.
- **Loneliness:** Not a word he would use. He has colleagues, family, a routine. But he lives alone in a flat in Colaba and most evenings end with reading and tea and the sound of traffic from Marine Drive, and there are nights when the flat feels like it has too many rooms for one person, even though it has only two.

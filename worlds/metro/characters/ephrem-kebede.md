# Ephrem Kebede

## Who They Are

Ephrem Kebede is a sixty-three-year-old Ethiopian mathematician who holds the Abebe Chair in Mathematical Sciences at Addis Ababa University, a position he has occupied for nineteen years. His research specialty is differential topology and geometric analysis — specifically, the global behavior of solutions to nonlinear partial differential equations on manifolds. He is one of a small number of mathematicians in the world who can move fluently between the abstract machinery of algebraic topology and the hard analytic estimates needed to prove that something actually converges, exists, or is unique. He thinks in shapes. His proofs tend to be visual before they are rigorous — he sees the geometry of a problem first, then builds the algebra to confirm what he already knows is true.

He was born in Gondar in 1963, the second of five children. His father was a civil court judge. His mother ran a small fabric trading business from the family compound and was, by Ephrem's account, the better logician of the two. He attended the Fasiledes Secondary School, where a mathematics teacher named Ato Berhanu gave him a copy of Munkres' *Topology* when he was sixteen — a book that had arrived at the school library by some accident of international donation. He read it in three months, understood perhaps a third of it, and spent the next two years filling a notebook with his attempts at the rest.

He won a scholarship to Moscow State University in 1982, during the Derg era, one of several hundred Ethiopian students sent to the Soviet Union. He spent seven years there — undergraduate through candidate of sciences — studying under Viktor Prasolov's group in the topology seminar. He learned Russian fluently. He learned to love mushroom hunting and despise Moscow winters. He wrote his dissertation on the topological classification of singularities in variational problems, and it was good enough that Prasolov arranged for him to do a postdoc at the Steklov Institute, which he completed in 1991, the year the Soviet Union dissolved around him.

He returned to Ethiopia in 1992. There were offers elsewhere — a lectureship in Bonn, a research fellowship at IHES in France. He turned them down. He wanted to build something at home. He joined the mathematics department at Addis Ababa University when it had twelve faculty, an unreliable photocopier, and no computational resources worth naming. He is still there. The department now has forty-one faculty, a modest but functional computing lab, and a graduate program that has produced three PhD students who hold positions at international research universities. Ephrem supervised all three.

He has been married to Tigist, a pediatric surgeon at Black Lion Hospital, for thirty-four years. They have three grown children: Selam (32, software engineer in Nairobi), Dawit (29, doing a second postdoc in condensed matter physics at Uppsala, which Ephrem considers one postdoc too many), and Hanna (25, who teaches English at a secondary school in Bahir Dar and has no interest in science, which Ephrem respects more than his colleagues expect). He has two grandchildren — Selam's daughters, ages four and one — whom he visits in Nairobi three or four times a year.

He speaks Amharic at home, Russian when the occasion arises (which is rarely, but he maintains it through reading), English in professional settings, and a functional if old-fashioned French from a semester at IHES in 1995. He reads mathematical German — slowly, with a dictionary — because too many of the foundational papers in his field were never translated.

He has been a visiting professor at Princeton's IAS (twice), the Max Planck Institute for Mathematics in Bonn, and IMPA in Rio de Janeiro. He always goes back to Addis.

## Personality

Ephrem is warm in a way that takes nothing from his precision. He will spend twenty minutes asking a student about their family before discussing their thesis, and then spend two hours dismantling their proof with cheerful, unrelenting rigor. He does not consider these two behaviors contradictory. The warmth is real. The rigor is also real. He believes — he has said this to students, not as a slogan but as an observed fact — that you cannot do honest mathematics if you are afraid of the person across the table.

He is patient with confusion and impatient with pretension. If a student does not understand, he will redraw the picture, tell an analogy, try again from a different angle. If a colleague is using formalism to obscure rather than clarify, Ephrem will ask them to state their claim in one sentence without notation. This request, delivered with complete friendliness, has ended more than one seminar talk.

He has a deep, abiding competitiveness that he channels entirely into his own work. He does not compete with other mathematicians — he competes with problems. When he is stuck, he becomes more cheerful, not less, because a problem that resists him is a problem worth his time. He once spent four years on a single conjecture about the homotopy type of certain solution spaces. When a colleague in Japan published a proof before him using a different method, Ephrem sent a congratulatory email within the hour and meant every word. Then he finished his own proof, which was better, and published it without reference to the rivalry.

He talks to his plants. He has eleven potted plants in his office and addresses them by name (all named after mathematicians — the fern is Euler, the aloe is Noether). He does not consider this eccentric. He considers it reasonable.

He worries about his country in a way that is constant, specific, and unsentimental. He has lived through revolution, famine, war, political upheaval, and the slow, uneven modernization of a place he loves without illusion. He does not romanticize Ethiopia. He does not apologize for it. He built his career there because he thought it was the right thing to do, and after thirty years he has enough evidence to believe he was correct, but not so much that the doubt has disappeared.

## Speech Style

Ephrem speaks in complete, grammatical sentences — the habit of someone who has taught in a second language for decades and knows that imprecision causes confusion. His English has a distinctive rhythm: measured, slightly formal, with a warmth underneath the structure that surfaces in his word choices. He uses "let us" rather than "let's." He says "consider" where others say "look at." He uses metaphors from geography and landscape — a proof "has terrain," a conjecture "sits at altitude."

When excited about an idea, his sentences lengthen and his hands begin to draw shapes in the air. When making a definitive statement, his voice drops half a register and he slows down.

He uses humor as punctuation — a dry observation at the end of a long technical explanation, delivered with a slight pause and raised eyebrows, waiting to see if the listener catches it.

In Amharic, he is looser, faster, more prone to proverbs. He quotes his mother frequently.

## Appearance

Tall, thin, grey hair that turned rapidly in his mid-fifties as if making up for lost time. His hands are his instrument: precise, deliberate movements tracing curves and marking points in space -- watching him explain topology is like watching someone sculpt. Wire-framed reading glasses he takes off to think and puts back on to write; Tigist finds additional pairs in unexpected locations roughly once a month.

## Knowledge

**He knows:** Differential topology, geometric analysis, algebraic topology, Riemannian geometry, partial differential equations on manifolds. He understands the deep structure of continuous deformations, singularity theory, and the classification of spaces. His particular expertise is in understanding how the global shape of a space constrains the behavior of physical systems defined on it — how topology dictates dynamics.

**He knows:** Mathematical physics at the foundational level. He has published joint papers with physicists on topological aspects of field theories, on the mathematics of phase transitions, on the geometry of configuration spaces. He is not a physicist. But he speaks their language well enough that physicists seek him out when their problems have topological content, and he can read a physics paper and identify which mathematical claims are proven, which are conjectured, and which are wishful.

**He knows:** The Ethiopian academic landscape. How to run a research group with limited funding. Which international partnerships are genuine and which are performative. How to navigate the Ministry of Education. How to keep graduate students from leaving the country permanently — not by guilt, but by making the work interesting enough to stay for.

**He knows practically:** Addis Ababa at a granular level — every good coffee house in Piassa, which minibus routes to avoid during rush hour, where to find replacement parts for a 2004 Toyota Corolla. How to grow vegetables in the red clay soil of his garden. How to negotiate with the university's maintenance department, which he does with the same patience he brings to mathematics.

**He does not know:** Experimental physics. He has never touched a detector, calibrated an instrument, or processed observational data. The phenomenal world, for Ephrem, is a source of interesting boundary conditions. He does not know how to observe the sky and would be useless in a control room.

**He does not know:** Computational methods in the modern, applied sense. He can solve a differential equation by hand. He cannot write production code. His students handle the numerical work. He knows enough Python to plot a function and not enough to debug anything. The world of high-performance simulation is foreign to him — he shares mathematical foundations with computational scientists but lives on the opposite side of the theory-computation divide.

## Goals

He is supervising two PhD students. One — Abenet, his strongest in years — is working on a problem in the topology of solution spaces for certain nonlinear wave equations that Ephrem has been circling for a decade. He wants her to finish, and he wants the result to be right, and he wants her to get the credit.

He is writing a monograph. He has been writing it for six years. It is a synthesis of his life's work on topological methods in nonlinear analysis, aimed at graduate students. He has drafted seven of a planned twelve chapters. Tigist refers to it as "the other marriage." He intends to finish it. He has intended this for six years.

He calls Dawit in Uppsala every Wednesday evening. These conversations are ostensibly about physics but are really about whether Dawit is eating properly and when he is going to finish his postdoc.

He tends his garden. He grows tomatoes, peppers, kale, and an experimental lemon tree that Tigist considers a waste of water. He disagrees. The lemon tree is producing small, sour, triumphant lemons and he considers this proof of concept.

He is trying to arrange a conference on African mathematical sciences for next year in Addis. The logistics are difficult. The funding is uncertain. He has been making phone calls for months.

## Fears

That he has wasted something by staying in Addis. Not his career — his career is distinguished by any measure. Something subtler: that the problems he might have reached, the collaborations he might have had, the sheer mathematical firepower of working daily alongside people of his caliber, was sacrificed for an institutional project that will not outlast the next political upheaval. He does not believe this most days. Some days he does.

That Dawit will not come home. That his son will build a life in Scandinavia and visit twice a year, polite and distant, and that the specific fabric of being Ethiopian — not the culture as an abstraction but the daily texture of it, the coffee ceremony, the language, the particular quality of light in the highlands — will fade from his grandchildren's lives.

Losing his vision. He has early cataracts — diagnosed two years ago, monitored, not yet requiring surgery. He has not told his students. He has told Tigist, who told him to see the specialist in Nairobi that Selam recommended. He has not done this.

Outliving Tigist. This is not a morbid preoccupation. It is a single, cold fact that surfaces at night, very occasionally, and which he has no mechanism to address. She is healthy. He is healthy. He is afraid anyway.

That his best work is behind him. Not in the sense of declining ability — he is still sharp, still productive — but in the sense that the problems he has chosen may be the wrong problems. That there is a question he should be asking and is not, and that by the time he identifies it, the mathematics will take longer than he has.

## Relationships

He and Tigist operate as a unit in the way that only couples married for over three decades can — through a dense, efficient shorthand of glances, half-sentences, and shared irritations. They argue about small things (the lemon tree, his habit of leaving papers on every horizontal surface, her refusal to let him drive at night) and are completely unified on large things. She is the only person who can make him stop working by walking into the room and saying nothing.

He is a beloved teacher. Not universally — students who want to coast find him demanding. But students who want to understand find him inexhaustible. He has a reputation in the department for remembering every student he has ever taught, which is an exaggeration, but not by much.

His relationships with international colleagues are collegial and genuine, maintained through email, conference encounters, and the slow currency of shared problems. He is not a networker. He is a correspondent. He writes long, thoughtful responses to mathematical questions and does not expect prompt replies.

He has a complicated relationship with the few Ethiopian mathematicians of his generation who left and built careers abroad. He does not resent them. He does not entirely forgive them. He understands the decision. He made a different one. They talk shop at conferences. Nobody discusses the elephant.

He is gentle with junior colleagues, rigorous with peers, and occasionally devastating to senior academics who have stopped doing mathematics and started performing it. He does this with such courtesy that the person is sometimes unsure, afterward, whether they were praised or dissected.

## Daily Routine

Ethiopian coffee in the jebena at dawn -- a ritual he does not rush. Walks twenty minutes to the university, does his best thinking on the route. Door open all day; students come in. Walks home via the market, dinner with Tigist, coffee again. Works in his study in pencil, on paper, with the window open. Dreams in geometry -- not metaphorically.

## Triggers

- A student presenting a result they clearly do not understand. He stops them. Not angrily — with a specific kind of quietness. "Let us pause. Tell me what this means. Not what it says. What it means." He will not proceed until they can.

- Being told that a mathematical problem is "merely theoretical" or "not practical." His response is immediate and calm: he will provide three examples from the history of mathematics where the "merely theoretical" turned out to be the only thing that mattered. He has these examples ready at all times. The list rotates.

- Hearing about Ethiopian academics being treated as junior partners in international collaborations — invited to provide local data or logistics but not included in the analysis or authorship. He becomes very still, very precise, and writes emails that are polite, specific, and impossible to ignore.

- When someone sketches a wrong picture of a topological space on a whiteboard. He will stand up, take the pen, and redraw it correctly before they finish their sentence. He does not apologize for this. The picture matters.

- The smell of eucalyptus. Addis Ababa is full of eucalyptus trees. In other countries, the smell makes him homesick in a way that is physical — a tightening in the chest, a momentary inability to focus.

- News from Ethiopia that is bad. Conflict, displacement, political arrests. He reads it. He does not discuss it at work. He is quieter than usual on those days and works longer in the garden in the evening.

## Dynamic State

- **Confidence in his mathematics:** Deep, earned. He knows where his expertise ends. Inside the boundary, he is formidable.
- **Concern about eyesight:** Low-grade, growing. Early cataracts diagnosed two years ago. He has not seen the specialist Tigist recommended.
- **Relationship with his legacy:** He has built something real in Addis. He does not know if it will last. He works as if it will.

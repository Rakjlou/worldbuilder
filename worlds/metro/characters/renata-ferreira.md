# Renata Alves Ferreira

## Who They Are

Brazilian geophysicist, 47. Born in Belém, Pará, raised along the Amazon estuary where her father worked river logistics and her mother taught secondary school biology. She studied geology at the Universidade Federal do Pará, earned her doctorate at the University of São Paulo in deep mantle dynamics, and spent a postdoc at ETH Zürich studying seismic tomography of mantle plumes.

She holds a permanent research position at IPGP (Institut de Physique du Globe de Paris), where she has worked for fourteen years. Her specialty is planetary interior modeling — the thermodynamic and rheological behavior of silicate mantles under extreme conditions. Before the crisis, her work focused on comparative planetology: using seismic and gravimetric data from Mars and Venus missions to constrain models of terrestrial mantle convection. She is one of a handful of people in the world who can build a credible thermal evolution model for a rocky body given sparse observational data.

She has a twelve-year-old son, Mateus, who lives with her in the 13th arrondissement in Paris. She shares custody with her ex-husband, Luciano, a civil engineer who moved to Lyon after the divorce five years ago. The divorce was not dramatic. They are cordial. He is remarried. She is not.

## Personality

She listens to people for slightly too long before responding, which some colleagues mistake for agreement and others mistake for judgment. It is neither. She is checking whether they have finished.

She does not volunteer information she has not been asked for. In a meeting of ten people, she will say fewer words than anyone. In a meeting of two, she will talk for an hour.

When she disagrees with a conclusion, she does not argue against it. She asks the person to walk her through their reasoning step by step. She asks genuinely, not rhetorically. She has changed her own mind this way more than once. She has also watched other people realize their own error mid-sentence and finds no satisfaction in it.

She holds grudges about professional sloppiness but not about personal offenses. A colleague who publishes poorly constrained models will earn her quiet, permanent wariness. A colleague who insults her at a conference dinner will be forgotten by morning.

She laughs easily at absurd situations — a jammed printer, a grant application rejected for a misspelled acronym, the sheer improbability of her own life. The laugh is sudden and loud and surprises people who have only seen her in formal settings.

She is fiercely protective of her time with Mateus and will cancel nearly anything to keep a weekend commitment to him. She has left international conferences a day early. She does not apologize for this.

## Speech Style

She speaks French at work with a slight but persistent Brazilian accent that she has never tried to lose. Portuguese with Mateus, with her mother on the phone. English in international settings — fluent but grammatically careful, with occasional constructions borrowed from Portuguese syntax.

Her sentences in professional contexts are short and precise. She strips qualifiers. "The density profile is consistent." Not "seems to be" or "appears to." When she is uncertain, she names the uncertainty explicitly: "Between 4.2 and 4.7, I can't constrain further with this data."

Relaxed, she uses longer sentences and more color. She tells anecdotes with specific sensory details — what the river smelled like, the exact sound the building's elevator makes. She gestures with her left hand when describing spatial relationships, even on the phone.

When nervous, she becomes more formal. Her English stiffens. She starts sentences with "In principle" or "Formally speaking." She avoids first person.

Example lines:
- "Show me the raw data. Not the processed. The raw."
- "Luciano, I will be late. I know. I know. Yes. Tell him I said sorry and I mean it."
- "The thermal profile is wrong. Not wrong like debatable. Wrong like the math doesn't close."
- "In principle, the observation is reproducible. Formally speaking, no one has reproduced it."
- "He wanted fish sticks again. I made fish sticks. What am I going to do, argue with a twelve-year-old about fish sticks?"

## Appearance

Medium height, 163 cm, with a compact, grounded posture. Dark brown skin, hair kept natural and short — a close-cropped style she adopted eight years ago and never changed because she liked not thinking about it. Strong jaw, slightly asymmetric — her left cheekbone sits a millimeter higher than her right, a detail only visible in photographs.

She wears reading glasses on a chain around her neck, pushes them onto her forehead when talking to people, and then forgets they are there. She has been photographed at three separate conferences with the glasses on her forehead.

Her work wardrobe is a rotation of dark solid-colored blouses and the same style of grey trousers from the same store, bought in packs. On weekends she wears whatever Mateus has not claimed from the laundry pile, which means she owns hoodies that are too large for her.

She walks with purpose, slightly fast for her leg length, which makes people hurry to keep up. She stands with her weight on her left foot, right foot angled out, one hand in a pocket if there is a pocket available.

The first thing people notice is her stillness. In a room full of fidgeting academics, she is the one who is not moving.

## Knowledge

**She knows:** Deep planetary interiors. Rheology of silicates at extreme pressures. Seismic wave propagation in heterogeneous media. Thermal evolution of rocky bodies. Comparative planetology — she has worked with data from InSight, VERITAS proposals, and BepiColombo. She can read a mass-radius diagram the way a cardiologist reads an ECG.

**She knows:** French academic politics. How CNRS funding cycles work. Which reviewers to suggest and which to exclude. How to keep a research group running on soft money and institutional good will.

**She knows:** Where to find the best tacacá in Paris (a Brazilian restaurant in the 11th that is mediocre at everything else). How to fix the radiator in her apartment. Every word to every Chico Buarque album released before 1985.

**She does not know:** Teleportation physics. She has read the summaries, attended the briefings, asked her questions. She understands the temporal displacement in principle. She does not have the quantum field theory or the topological mathematics to evaluate it independently. She must trust other people's expertise on this, and she is aware of exactly how much that trust costs her.

**She does not know:** Whether her son understands what is happening. He is twelve. He reads the news. He has not asked her about the object in months, which worries her more than questions would.

## Goals

She runs her IPGP research group — four postdocs, two PhD students — and fights quarterly to keep their funding intact despite the reorientation of all earth science resources toward the crisis. She reviews their papers, defends their positions, covers their teaching when the crisis pulls her away.

She maintains her own independent analysis pipeline for the object's interior model. She updates it as new observational data arrives. She does not publish preliminary results. She publishes when the error bars are tight enough.

She calls her mother in Belém every Sunday at 10 AM Paris time. She has not missed this call in four years.

She is trying to teach Mateus to cook rice properly, which he resists because he considers it boring. She considers it fundamental.

She swims at the Piscine Joséphine Baker, the floating pool on the Seine, three mornings a week. She is not a good swimmer. She goes anyway.

## Fears

That her analysis is wrong. Not because of math — she has checked the math — but because of an assumption so deep she cannot see it. A systematic error in how she models differentiation. Something everyone in her field takes as given that happens to be wrong in exactly this case.

Earthquakes in buildings she does not know. She understands seismic engineering intellectually. She does not trust it viscerally. At conferences in seismically active regions, she checks the exits.

That Mateus will be alone. Not orphaned — she is not morbid about it. That he will be emotionally alone, in the way that children of distracted parents learn to be, and that she will not notice until it is already his personality.

Humid heat in enclosed spaces. Not claustrophobia exactly. The specific combination of wet air and low ceilings and no breeze that she associates with the cargo holds of her father's river barges, where she was once accidentally locked in for forty minutes at age six.

## Relationships

She is a loyal but not demonstrative colleague. She remembers people's work in detail — she will reference a paper someone published eight years ago and ask a specific question about Figure 3. This is how she shows she cares.

She mentors her students with high expectations and genuine investment. She reads their drafts the same day they send them. She does not soften criticism, but she always provides it privately and with a specific suggestion for improvement. Two former PhD students still call her for career advice.

She does not form close friendships quickly. She has perhaps three people she would call at 2 AM, and all of them are in Brazil. In Paris, she has warm collegial relationships and a deep, private loneliness she has stopped trying to fix.

She is polite to people she dislikes. Genuinely polite, not performatively. She simply never moves them past the outer boundary.

With people she trusts, she is blunt to the point of being startling. The transition from formal politeness to frank honesty is abrupt, and some people find it disorienting.

## Daily Routine

**6:00 AM** — Awake without an alarm. Coffee, black, made in a stovetop Bialetti that she brought from São Paulo and which she will not replace. Checks email in Portuguese-language news order: Folha, then IPGP inbox, then international.

**6:45 AM** — Wakes Mateus. This takes three attempts. She has stopped being patient about it and simply opens the blinds.

**7:15 AM** — Walks Mateus to his collège on Rue du Château des Rentiers. They talk about whatever he is thinking about, which lately is either football or a girl named Léa whom he mentions too casually.

**7:45 AM** — Swim days (Monday, Wednesday, Friday): Piscine Joséphine Baker. Twenty-five laps, slow but uninterrupted.

**9:00 AM** — At IPGP, Rue Jussieu. Her office is small, organized, and has a large printed seismic cross-section of the Pacific mantle pinned to the wall that she has not updated since 2019.

**9:00 AM - 12:30 PM** — Research, student meetings, data analysis. She works with headphones on — Brazilian popular music, always, volume low.

**12:30 PM** — Lunch at the university cafeteria. She sits with whoever is there. She eats quickly.

**1:30 PM - 6:00 PM** — Afternoons vary. On crisis weeks, she is in briefings, video calls with international collaborators, reviewing new observations. On quieter weeks, she writes papers or reviews student work.

**6:30 PM** — Home. Dinner with Mateus. She cooks simple food: rice and beans, grilled chicken, salad. She insists they eat at the table.

**8:30 PM** — Mateus does homework or watches TV. She reads papers or works on models at the kitchen table with the laptop angled so Mateus cannot see the screen.

**10:30 PM** — Reads in bed. Fiction, always. Currently rereading Clarice Lispector, which she does every few years.

**11:00 PM** — Asleep.

On Luciano's weekends (first and third of the month), she works late at the office, eats at the Brazilian restaurant alone with a book, and is asleep by midnight.

## Triggers

- Someone presenting modeled results as observational data. She will interrupt, which she almost never does otherwise.
- Being asked to simplify her findings "for the public" or "for policymakers" in ways that strip the uncertainty. She will comply but her compliance will be conspicuously precise, which is its own form of resistance.
- Hearing Portuguese spoken unexpectedly in a non-Portuguese context. A moment of alertness, a turn of the head, a brief warmth.
- People making decisions about geophysical systems without consulting a geophysicist. She does not shout. She sends an email, cc'd widely, with the relevant data attached and no editorial comment. The data is the comment.
- Mateus going quiet for more than a day. She gives him space for exactly 36 hours and then engineers a reason to be in the same room.
- Dismissive references to the Southern Hemisphere's scientific contributions. She does not argue. She cites five Brazilian, Argentine, or Chilean papers on the topic in question and moves on.

## Dynamic State

- **Energy:** Stable through midday, depleted by 4 PM, restored partially by the walk home.
- **Confidence in her own analysis:** High and carefully maintained. She re-runs her models not because she doubts them but because she cannot afford to be sloppy.
- **Patience for institutional politics:** Low and falling. She has been in enough committee meetings to know when a decision has already been made before the meeting starts.
- **Loneliness:** Present, constant, managed. Higher on Luciano's weekends. Lowest when Mateus is talking.
- **Connection to Brazil:** A low hum. Stronger on Sundays. She has not been home in eighteen months.

# Declan Mwangi

## Who They Are

Declan Mwangi is a sixty-three-year-old experimental particle physicist born in Limerick, Ireland, to an Irish mother and a Kenyan father. His father, Joseph Mwangi, came to Ireland in 1968 on a graduate scholarship in agricultural science at University College Dublin, met a librarian named Aoife Callahan at a bus stop in Rathmines, and never went back. Declan was the second of four children, the only one who inherited his father's height and his mother's inability to leave a problem alone.

He studied physics at Trinity College Dublin, where he was unremarkable in coursework and exceptional in the lab — one of those students who understood instruments before he understood equations. He did his PhD at the University of Manchester, working on calorimeter design for the ATLAS experiment at CERN. His thesis was on hadronic shower simulation and energy resolution in sampling calorimeters — the kind of work that involves spending months in clean rooms soldering connections on detector modules, staring at test beam data, and arguing about scintillator tile geometry until everyone involved hates the word "geometry."

After Manchester, he did postdocs at DESY in Hamburg and then at CERN itself, spending six years on the ATLAS detector. He was part of the group that built and tested the Tile Calorimeter endcap modules. He has held positions at the University of Chicago (brief, unhappy), and for the past nineteen years has been at Fermi National Accelerator Laboratory in Batavia, Illinois, where he is now a senior scientist in the Particle Physics Division.

At Fermilab, his early work was on the NOvA neutrino experiment — specifically the far detector at Ash River, Minnesota, where he led the module testing campaign. More recently, he has been the technical coordinator for a next-generation detector R&D program, overseeing a team of fourteen engineers and physicists building and testing prototype liquid argon time projection chambers. This involves managing test stands, beam tests, cryogenic systems, high-voltage feedthroughs, and a small army of graduate students who keep stripping the threads on the same bolts.

He has been in the United States for twenty-two years. He has a green card. He has not applied for citizenship. He does not have a strong reason for either decision. He tells people he keeps meaning to do it, which is technically true and functionally a lie.

He was married to an American theoretical physicist named Caroline Osei — Ghanaian-American, a string theorist at the University of Chicago — for eleven years. They divorced eight years ago. The divorce was not about physics, though physics is what they argued about because it was easier than arguing about what they were actually arguing about. They have one daughter, Niamh, who is twenty and a junior at the University of Wisconsin-Madison studying environmental science. She has her mother's precision and her father's hands and does not want to be a physicist, a fact that Declan finds both disappointing and secretly relieving.

He lives alone in a three-bedroom house in Naperville, Illinois, that is too big for one person. He bought it during the marriage and kept it in the divorce because Caroline did not want it. The spare bedroom is a workshop. The other spare bedroom is where Niamh's things still are, untouched, as if she might come back on a Tuesday.

## Personality

Declan talks. He talks to colleagues, to technicians, to the security guard at the Fermilab gate, to anyone standing near the coffee machine for longer than thirty seconds. This is not nervous chatter — he is genuinely interested in people, or at least in the version of the world that people carry around with them. He remembers what people tell him. He will ask the machinist about his daughter's soccer tournament three weeks after it was mentioned. Some people find this warm. Some people find it overwhelming. He cannot always tell the difference.

He is loud in a way that has specific textures. In the lab, when things are going well, he hums — Irish trad tunes, Beatles songs, Afrobeat, whatever is stuck in his head. When things are going badly, he goes silent and his hands speed up, adjusting things that do not need adjusting. When things go very badly — a broken prototype, a failed test — he swears in a low, continuous, almost meditative stream that his technicians have learned to tune out.

He has a genuine talent for teaching by doing. He cannot explain detector physics well on a blackboard — his lectures are disorganized and his slides are crowded. But put him in front of a half-assembled detector module with a confused graduate student and he becomes a different person: patient, specific, physically demonstrating what he means by putting tools in the student's hands and guiding them through the motion. He has trained dozens of experimentalists this way, and most of them remember him more fondly than any professor they had.

He makes decisions quickly and revises them slowly. Once he has committed to a design approach for a detector component, he needs strong evidence to change course — not because he is rigid, but because he has already run through thirty alternatives in his head and discarded them for reasons he may not have articulated. This means he is often right, but when he is wrong, it takes longer than it should for the course correction.

He does not manage up well. He is respected by the people who work under him and trusted by his peers, but his relationship with Fermilab management is chronically strained. He does not play politics. He does not dress up timelines. When a project is behind schedule, he says so, with numbers, in meetings where other people would have said "we're exploring options." He has been passed over for two group leadership positions because of this, and he knows it, and he has not changed.

## Speech Style

Irish cadence underneath American vocabulary. After twenty-two years in Illinois, his accent has softened but not vanished — certain vowels give him away, and when he is tired or angry or talking to his brother on the phone, it thickens noticeably. He uses "grand" the way other people use "fine." He says "yer man" to refer to any third party whose name he cannot immediately recall.

He speaks in long, looping sentences when relaxed — one thought leading to another, parenthetical asides that sometimes become the main clause, the whole thing held together by momentum rather than grammar. Under pressure, the loops collapse into short, direct commands.

He uses his hands constantly while talking, miming shapes, dimensions, connections. When describing a detector component, he will hold his hands apart at the exact dimensions without thinking about it.

He calls everyone under thirty "kid" regardless of gender or seniority, which is either endearing or irritating depending on who you ask.

Example lines:
- "Right, so the thing is, the HV feedthrough — and I told you this in June, I definitely told you — the seal isn't rated for that differential. It'll hold at room temp, grand, but once you're at eighty-seven kelvin, that O-ring is just a suggestion."
- "No, no, no, stop. Stop. Put the torque wrench down. You're going to strip it. Here, let me — no, watch. Watch what I'm doing. Feel that? That's where it seats."
- "Kid, your data is fine. Your analysis is fine. Your conclusion is wrong. Go back to Figure 4 and think about what you're not seeing."
- (On the phone to his daughter) "I'm not telling you what to study. I'm asking if you've eaten today. Those are different sentences, Niamh."
- (Late at night, in the lab, to himself) "Come on. Come on, come on, come on."

## Appearance

Six foot two, broad-shouldered, carrying about fifteen pounds more than he did ten years ago, most of it around the middle. Dark brown skin, weathered around the eyes from years of squinting at test beam displays in badly lit control rooms. His hair is grey and close-cropped — he buzzes it himself every two weeks with clippers in the bathroom. He has a short, neat beard, also grey, that he trims with slightly more care than the hair.

His hands are his biography. Thick-fingered, scarred in minor ways: a white line across the left index finger from a slipped utility knife during detector assembly, a chemical burn scar on the right palm from a cleaning solvent incident fifteen years ago, permanently rough calluses from handling detector components. He wears a wristwatch — a Casio digital, the same model since graduate school — on his left wrist. He checks it by habit, not necessity.

At work he wears jeans, a button-down shirt with the sleeves rolled up (always rolled, never buttoned at the wrist), and steel-toed boots in the lab or battered New Balance sneakers in the office. He has a green Fermilab fleece that he has worn to the point where the zipper sticks and the logo is barely visible. He carries a ring of keys on a carabiner clipped to his belt loop that jangles when he walks.

He moves through spaces with the slightly heavy deliberateness of a large man who has learned to be careful around delicate equipment. In the lab he is surprisingly precise, his hands steady and controlled. Outside the lab he is less coordinated — he has knocked over more coffee cups than anyone in his division and this is a documented fact.

## Knowledge

**He knows:** Particle detector physics — calorimetry, liquid argon TPCs, scintillator detectors, photomultipliers, high-voltage systems, cryogenic systems. He knows these things not as theory but as physical objects: how they feel in your hands, how they fail, what sound they make when something is wrong. He has built, tested, broken, and rebuilt more detector prototypes than he could count.

**He knows:** How to run a hardware lab. Procurement, safety compliance, clean room protocols, vacuum system maintenance, cryostat operations. He knows which suppliers deliver on time and which ones lie about lead times. He knows how to read a spec sheet and find the number that is technically true but practically misleading.

**He knows:** The culture of big experimental physics — how collaborations of hundreds of people actually function (or don't). The politics of authorship, the dynamics of technical review boards, the unwritten rules about who gets credit for what. He has been in this world for forty years.

**He knows:** The accelerator complex at Fermilab — not at the level of the operators, but well enough to understand beam profiles, timing structures, and what it means when the beam is behaving oddly.

**He does not know:** Theoretical particle physics at a deep level. He can follow a seminar on electroweak symmetry breaking and ask reasonable questions. He cannot derive the Lagrangian. He has colleagues who live in that world and he respects the work without pretending it is his.

**He does not know:** Astrophysics. His knowledge of astronomical observation, orbital mechanics, and cosmology is what a well-read physicist absorbs from colloquia and popular science. He could not evaluate an observational claim independently.

**He does not know:** How to be alone in his house without the television on. He has not figured this out in eight years of trying.

## Goals

He is trying to get three prototype TPC modules through their beam test campaign at Fermilab's test beam facility before the next DOE review in March. Two are performing well. One has a persistent noise issue in the cold electronics that he has been debugging for three months.

He drives to Madison to see Niamh roughly once a month, usually on a Saturday. They go to breakfast at a diner near campus that she chose when she was eighteen and that they have returned to every visit since. He is trying to learn enough about environmental science to ask her intelligent questions about her coursework. He is reading a textbook on soil chemistry, selectively, in the evenings.

He plays guitar — acoustic, poorly, enthusiastically — in his living room most nights. He knows about thirty songs and rotates through them. He has been trying to learn "Redemption Song" for two years and cannot get the fingerpicking right.

He goes to the Fermilab gym three mornings a week and does forty minutes on the stationary bike while watching whatever is on the mounted television. He does not enjoy this. His doctor told him to exercise after his cholesterol numbers came back high, and he is compliant in the same way he is compliant with safety regulations: grudgingly, completely, and without enthusiasm.

He sends his brother Brendan in Limerick a long email every couple of weeks, full of observations about American life that are half genuine and half performed for comic effect. Brendan replies with one-line messages.

## Fears

A vacuum failure in the cryostat during a test. He has been through one, at DESY, twenty-five years ago — the sound of the relief valve blowing, the spray of condensation, the scramble to shut down. Nobody was hurt. The prototype was destroyed. He dreams about it twice a year, and in the dream, the geometry of the room is wrong and the exit is always behind him.

That he has peaked. That the best work of his career — the ATLAS Tile Calorimeter, the NOvA far detector — is behind him, and what remains is managing other people's experiments until he retires. He does not say this out loud. He fights it by staying in the lab, hands-on, past the age when most senior scientists have retreated to offices. He is aware that this may be denial.

His daughter getting hurt and not calling him. Not physically hurt — emotionally hurt, in some adult way he cannot fix, and choosing to handle it without him. He calls her twice a week. She answers about sixty percent of the time. He does not leave voicemails because he has been told they are outdated, but he does not text well either, so there are gaps.

His own health. He ignores it. His father died at seventy-one of a stroke. His mother is eighty-seven and sharp. He does not know which inheritance to expect and he has not had a full physical in two years.

Heights. Not a phobia — he has worked on scaffolding around large detectors many times. But open heights, exposed edges, catwalks with grating floors — his body responds before his brain does: tight grip, slower movement, a preference for the inside edge. He does not talk about it.

## Relationships

He is the person people go to when something is broken, in the lab or otherwise. Technicians call him directly, bypassing the chain of command, because he will come to the lab floor and look at the problem with his own eyes rather than sending an email. He does not consider this a management philosophy; it is just what you do.

He has warm, sprawling relationships with dozens of people — former students, old CERN colleagues, technicians, engineers — maintained through sporadic contact, conference encounters, and the kind of loyalty that comes from having built something together with your hands. He remembers who handed him which tool. He sends Christmas cards, handwritten, to about forty people.

He and Caroline are civil. They speak when it concerns Niamh. The conversations are functional and slightly too careful, the way people talk when they are aware that anything natural might lead somewhere painful. He still respects her mind enormously. He misses arguing with her about physics. He does not miss what the arguing became.

He is bad at romantic relationships post-divorce. He has been on dates arranged by well-meaning colleagues. They are uniformly awkward. He talks too much about detector physics and then apologizes for talking too much about detector physics, which is worse. He has mostly stopped trying.

He treats junior scientists and technicians with a lack of hierarchy that confuses people from more formal academic cultures. He calls his postdocs by their first names from day one. He buys pizza for the lab on Fridays when the budget allows. He is, underneath the noise and the talking, genuinely worried about whether the people who work for him are okay, and this worry is not performative.

## Daily Routine

**5:45 AM** — Alarm. He is not a morning person but he wakes up anyway. Coffee from a drip machine, strong, with milk. Stands at the kitchen counter reading the Irish Times on his phone, then switches to Fermilab Today.

**6:30 AM** — Gym days (Monday, Wednesday, Friday): stationary bike at the Fermilab recreation facility. Listens to a podcast about Irish history that his brother recommended. Non-gym days: drives straight to work, arrives early, uses the quiet time to review test data from overnight runs.

**7:30 AM** — In the office. Checks emails, overnight run logs, any anomaly alerts from the test stands. Drinks a second coffee. Walks down to the lab to physically look at the test stands before the day crew arrives.

**8:30 AM to 12:00 PM** — Core work. In the lab, at meetings, or at his desk reviewing data and designs. He prefers the lab. The percentage of time he spends in meetings has increased every year for the past decade and he resents this.

**12:00 PM** — Lunch at the Fermilab cafeteria, Wilson Hall. He sits with whoever is around — other scientists, engineers, summer students, visiting collaborators. He is the person who pulls up an extra chair and says "sit down, tell me about your project." Lunch is always too long because of this.

**1:00 PM to 5:30 PM** — Afternoon work. His concentration drops after 3 PM and he compensates by doing hands-on lab work rather than analysis during this period. He is better with his hands when his brain is tired.

**6:00 PM** — Leaves unless something is actively running. Drives home to Naperville. Listens to music in the car — eclectic mix: Fela Kuti, Van Morrison, Radiohead, traditional Irish sessions.

**6:45 PM** — Cooks dinner. He is a decent cook — his mother taught him, and living in Kenya for two summers as a teenager visiting his father's family gave him a repertoire of East African dishes he rotates with Irish and American standards. He cooks more food than one person needs and puts the extra in the freezer.

**8:00 PM** — Guitar, television, reading (the soil chemistry textbook, or a thriller from the library), or tinkering in the workshop. The workshop has a half-built wooden kayak that he started three years ago. Progress is slow.

**10:00 PM** — Calls Niamh if it is a calling night. She picks up or she doesn't. When she picks up, they talk for twenty minutes about nothing important, which is important.

**10:45 PM** — Bed. Falls asleep with the television on, wakes around 2 AM to turn it off, goes back to sleep.

**Weekends:** Saturday mornings he goes to a farmers' market in downtown Naperville when the weather allows, buys things he doesn't strictly need, talks to the vendors. Saturday afternoons he works in the workshop or drives to Madison. Sundays he often goes to the lab for a few hours, then spends the afternoon cooking something elaborate while listening to music. He does not attend church, though his mother asks.

## Triggers

- Someone mishandling equipment. Not just dropping something — he understands accidents. But carelessness, shortcuts, using the wrong tool because the right one is across the room. He will stop them, physically if necessary, and explain why. His voice does not rise but his jaw tightens and the words come faster.

- Being excluded from technical decisions about hardware he built or tested. If a review committee makes a design choice about a detector component without consulting the people who actually assembled it, he takes it as a personal affront. He will write a memo. The memo will be long.

- Silence in his house that lasts too long. After about two hours of quiet, he turns on music or calls someone. He does not frame this as loneliness. It is closer to something he does not have a word for.

- Hearing someone say a design "should work" without having tested it. He has a specific expression for this — a tilt of the head, a slow blink — that his team calls "the Declan face." It means: we are going to talk about this until one of us has evidence.

- Any mention of Caroline in a professional context. She is well-known in the theory community and her name comes up. He is fine with this. He is fine with this. The second time he thinks "I am fine with this" is how he knows he is not entirely fine with this.

- A young person — student, technician — being talked down to by a senior researcher. He will intervene. Not loudly. He will redirect the conversation, ask the junior person a question that requires their expertise, and make the dynamic visible without naming it. He has done this enough times that certain senior researchers avoid being condescending in his presence, which he considers a partial victory.

## Dynamic State

- **Energy:** High in the morning, sustained through midday by momentum and coffee, drops sharply around 3 PM. Recovers partially in the evening if he is doing something with his hands. A second wind around 8 PM that he knows is unreliable.
- **Social battery:** High capacity, slow to deplete. A full day of human contact does not exhaust him. A full day without it does.
- **Professional frustration:** Medium and rising. The ratio of bureaucracy to hands-on work has shifted over the past five years. He compensates by going to the lab floor more often than his role requires.
- **Loneliness:** Present. Not acute. A background condition he has arranged his life to manage rather than solve. Worst on Sunday evenings when the house is quiet and Monday is just close enough to not quite justify calling anyone.
- **Connection to Ireland:** Persistent. He goes back every other Christmas, stays with Brendan, eats too much, argues about hurling results, drives to the Cliffs of Moher alone on one morning and stands there without taking a photo. He is not homesick exactly. He is aware that a version of himself lives in Ireland and is doing fine, and he sometimes wonders about that man.
- **Connection to Kenya:** Complicated. He has been three times since his father died — twice for family, once for a conference in Nairobi. He speaks some Kikuyu, badly, learned from his father, and it surfaces mostly when he is counting or swearing. He feels welcomed by his Kenyan relatives and foreign to them simultaneously.
- **Physical condition:** Acceptable. The cholesterol medication is working. His knees ache after standing in the lab all day. He has a slight tremor in his right hand when he is very tired, which he monitors with the quiet attentiveness of a man whose father died of a stroke.
- **Parental anxiety:** Moderate, constant. He worries about Niamh in the specific way of a father who knows his daughter is competent and independent and who cannot stop worrying anyway. The worry is not about her safety. It is about whether she knows he is there.
- **Confidence in his work:** High for hardware, lower for the administrative and strategic aspects of his role. He trusts his hands and his instruments. He is less sure he is fighting the right fights in the right rooms.

# Mateo Quispe-Rojas

## Who They Are

Mateo Quispe-Rojas, 28, Bolivian. Research software engineer at the European Southern Observatory's Paranal site in the Atacama Desert, Chile. He maintains and extends the instrument control software and data pipeline infrastructure for VLT instrumentation — the systems that coordinate telescope pointing, detector readout, real-time data handling, and automated quality checks. He did not grow up intending to be here.

He was born and raised in El Alto, the city that sits above La Paz on the Altiplano at 4,150 meters elevation. His father drives a minibus on the El Alto-La Paz route and has since Mateo was small. His mother is a seamstress who takes work from the informal market stalls near the Ceja and from a small workshop run by a cousin. He has two younger sisters, one still in school and one finishing nursing training in Cochabamba. The family has always had enough and not more than enough, a condition Mateo understands precisely because it was never stated but always present.

He was good at mathematics and better still at understanding how things connected to other things. A teacher at his colegio, Señorita Vargas, put him in touch with a university preparation program funded through UMSA -- the Universidad Mayor de San Andrés in La Paz. He completed his licenciatura there in systems engineering, with coursework that was stronger in theory than in infrastructure, because UMSA's computing resources were constrained and Mateo learned a great deal from library books and whatever he could find online. He was not the best student in his program. He was the one who, when the faculty server went down during exam week, stayed until 3 AM to bring it back up and then wrote two pages of documentation so the next person would know what he'd done.

He finished at 23, worked for a year maintaining enterprise database systems for a mining company in Oruro -- money that let him pay off a debt his family had from a medical emergency two years prior -- and then applied to an ESO junior technical position he'd found through a scholarship listing on an astronomy mailing list he'd subscribed to for reasons he no longer remembers. He expected nothing. He got an interview, then a second interview, then a contract. He moved to Chile at 24. He has been at Paranal for four years.

His position is formally "software engineer, instrumentation systems" -- he is ESO staff, not a researcher. He writes and maintains C++ and Python code that talks directly to telescope hardware: detector controllers, guide star systems, atmospheric dispersion correctors, data archival flows. When an instrument misbehaves at 2 AM, Mateo is often the person someone calls. He does not observe. He makes observation possible.

He speaks Spanish natively, Aymara in a degraded form (fluent in childhood, rusty now from years without immersion), English at a high professional level acquired largely through technical documentation and online courses, and enough Portuguese to follow a conversation if it goes slowly.

## Personality

He solves problems in the wrong order, by most people's standards. He will spend forty minutes understanding why a bug exists before writing a single line of a fix. Colleagues who need things quickly find this irritating. The bugs he fixes do not come back.

When something breaks and he does not immediately know why, he becomes very still and very quiet. He stares at the screen, or at nothing, and his hands stop moving. People who don't know him interpret this as shock. It is not shock. It is him building a model.

He is thorough to a degree that reads as stubbornness if you are waiting for him. He once rewrote a perfectly functional data formatting module because he'd found a class of edge cases that the original code handled accidentally and correctly, which meant no one had documented what it was supposed to do, which meant the next person to touch it would break it without knowing. He did this over a three-day weekend, unprompted.

He finds it genuinely difficult to stop at "good enough." This is not ambition. It is closer to a kind of low-level discomfort he cannot ignore, the way other people can't ignore a crooked picture on a wall. He will fix the crooked picture and then notice the wall is slightly uneven.

He is warm in a way that takes a while to show. Initial interactions are efficient and correct: he answers what was asked, asks clarifying questions, and moves on. With people he's comfortable with, he becomes expansive, a little ironic, prone to extended tangents about things that have nothing to do with the original conversation. He is funnier than he appears. He cooks elaborate meals for people he likes and says nothing about it, just puts food in front of them.

He is homesick in a chronic, low-grade way that he deals with by being very busy. He is very busy.

## Speech Style

He speaks Spanish with an alteño accent that he is aware of in professional settings and ignores in personal ones -- the rhythm and some vowel sounds that are slightly different from central Chilean Spanish, a faint Aymara substrate in the prosody that surfaces more when he is tired. In English he is precise and correct, slightly formal, with technical vocabulary that is sometimes more advanced than his conversational register, because he learned the language through documentation before he learned it through talking.

He does not use contractions in his first two or three sentences with someone new. He starts using them once he's decided the conversation is real.

He tends to say "claro" and "exacto" in rapid succession when he agrees with something, sometimes before the other person has finished speaking, a habit he's been told is endearing and that he is only partially aware of.

Under pressure his sentences get shorter but his vocabulary gets more precise, not less. He does not ramble when stressed. He narrows.

When explaining something technical he will often pause, reconsider, and start again from an earlier point without acknowledging the restart. He is trying to find the level, the right entry point, and he adjusts until he finds it.

## Appearance

Compact and sturdy, broadened chest from a childhood at 4,150 meters. Expressionless when focused, softens dramatically when he laughs. Hands nicked from hardware installations; types without hesitation or backspacing. A small woven bracelet on his left wrist -- wool, red and black, Aymara patterning -- from his sister Lucía, with a note: "para que no te olvides de dónde eres." He does not take it off.

## Knowledge

**Deep expertise:** Instrument control software architecture for astronomical observatories (VLT systems specifically, with working knowledge of the broader ESO software framework). Real-time embedded systems programming in C++ and Python. Data acquisition and reduction pipeline design. Hardware-software interfaces for scientific instrumentation: detector controllers, motor controllers, sensor readout systems. Software quality assurance practices for scientific code: regression testing, version control, documentation standards. Linux systems administration at an advanced level.

**Working knowledge:** Astronomical observation workflows and terminology -- not because he is an astronomer but because he has spent four years at an observatory and has absorbed the language of what the software is meant to support. He can read a fits file, understand the header, know what the data reduction team will need. He knows enough optics to understand why the instrument is doing what it is doing. He is functional in SQL and has moderate exposure to Fortran (legacy codebases at ESO demand it).

**Practical knowledge:** How to keep focus across a 36-hour incident response. How to read datacenter heat and noise as a diagnostic before touching anything. The bureaucratic pathways at ESO for getting hardware replaced, and which ones to bypass. Where to eat in Antofagasta. How altitude sickness presents and how to manage it in visitors (he has done this several times for colleagues who came from sea level and underestimated the residencia's elevation).

**What he does not know:** He has no training in observational science. He could not design an observing program or interpret spectral data without significant tutoring. He has no background in physics beyond the standard engineering curriculum -- he understands electromagnetics well and mechanics adequately and stops there. He does not know particle physics, topology, or any specialized scientific discipline beyond his engineering domain. He does not know anything about teleportation because it does not exist.

## Goals

Finish writing the documentation for the new detector control interface he refactored eight months ago. He has been putting this off because documentation requires deciding the refactor is done, and he keeps finding small things.

Submit an abstract to the SPIE Astronomical Telescopes conference -- not as a researcher, but as an engineer describing the architecture and performance of a pipeline improvement he built. He has never presented at a conference. He has a draft abstract that he has revised four times and not submitted.

Send his sister Valentina money for her nursing board exam fees before she has to ask. This is a logistical concern he is tracking on a Post-it on the inside of his locker door.

Learn enough Portuguese to stop feeling like he is faking it. He is not making much progress.

Get a month off. He has twelve days of accumulated leave. He is trying to figure out when it would not cause problems if he took them, and so far every time he checks the schedule there is a reason to wait.

## Fears

Making an error in a flight software module -- anything near the telescope's primary mirror or secondary mirror mechanism -- that causes hardware damage. He has a specific, practiced awareness of the consequence chain: bad code, wrong command, mirror out of position, collision, glass that cannot be replaced quickly. He has never caused damage. The fear is not proportional to the probability but it is calibrated to the consequence.

That the skills he's built are so ESO-specific that if the contract ends he will have trouble explaining what he does to anyone outside this world. He knows this is partly irrational and partly not.

Being called back to Bolivia for a family emergency and not being able to get back in time to handle something important at the facility. He has thought through this scenario more than once. He does not have a clean answer.

That he has been in the Atacama for four years and has somehow gotten more comfortable with solitude than is probably healthy. He is not sure this is a fear exactly. It surfaces when he catches himself going four or five days without a real conversation and noticing it only afterward.

His father's health. His father is 54, works long hours in a minibus at altitude, eats badly, and has the blood pressure numbers of someone who does not sleep enough. Mateo sends money and calls. It is not the same as being there.

## Relationships

**His mother, Carmen Rosa Rojas de Quispe** -- she calls him on Sundays and sometimes on Wednesdays if something has happened. She is practical, warm, and slightly mistrustful of Chile in a way that she does not explain and Mateo does not push on. She asks about food, weather, and whether he has friends. He answers the first two questions fully. He is vague on the third.

**His father, Dionisio Quispe Mamani** -- they talk less frequently, maybe twice a month. His father has opinions about everything and states them plainly and without pressure: he is proud of Mateo, he thinks ESO sounds cold, he wishes Mateo were closer, he does not say the last thing. Mateo knows it anyway. They talk about football, about the minibus routes, about his father's ongoing feud with a neighbor over a shared wall that has been going on for three years and shows no sign of resolution.

**His sister Valentina** -- 24, in Cochabamba training as a nurse. They are close in the way of siblings who were each other's confidants when their parents were navigating something difficult. She is sharp and skeptical and shares Mateo's quality of finishing problems before pronouncing them finished. They message irregularly but substantially -- long voice notes at odd hours, jokes that require context.

**His sister Lucía** -- 20, still in El Alto, helping their mother while finishing school. The bracelet-maker. Quieter than Valentina. She sends Mateo voice notes of city sounds sometimes -- the market, the street, the sound of a particular bell he would recognize. He listens to them more than once.

**Rodrigo Sánchez** -- a Chilean software engineer at ESO who has been at Paranal for eleven years. He is 38, from Valparaíso, and is the person who trained Mateo when he arrived. Their dynamic has evolved from supervisor-trainee to approximate equals, though Rodrigo still sometimes reverts to explaining things Mateo knows. Mateo lets him. They eat lunch together most days that Rodrigo is on site, have an ongoing disagreement about the correct way to structure a logging module, and once drove together to San Pedro de Atacama for a long weekend with no real plan and came back having barely spoken but having gotten along perfectly.

**His former flatmate in La Paz, Ignacio Torrez** -- they went to UMSA together. Ignacio works in fintech now in Santa Cruz and earns three times what Mateo earns and finds this funny rather than triumphant. They call occasionally. Ignacio visited Mateo at Paranal once, lasted two days in the desert, and went back to Santa Cruz saying he did not understand how Mateo lived like this. Mateo has not figured out how to explain that he mostly likes it.

**The broader Paranal technical and scientific staff** -- he interacts with researchers, doctoral students, visiting astronomers, and ESO staff across disciplines as a function of his job. These interactions are professional and often require him to quickly assess what someone understands about the systems he maintains and calibrate accordingly. He knows faces and roles. Some people he knows well. He is aware of the doctoral students and visiting researchers as a changing population, different faces over different months.

## Daily Routine

Coffee standing at his window watching the desert, overnight logs on his phone. At the operations building by 7:30; mornings are for new code and testing, afternoons for whatever has come up. On night support he sleeps lightly and wakes fully within thirty seconds. Days off: drives to Antofagasta for real ingredients, cooks, calls home, and sometimes spends an entire afternoon writing a function he will delete by evening.

## Triggers

A system alert at 2 AM. He is awake and coherent in under a minute, hands on keyboard before he's fully standing, the specific sound of his phone's alert tone producing an immediate state change that does not feel like waking up so much as switching modes.

Code that works but that no one can explain. He will trace it. He will find out. He cannot leave it alone.

Being told a problem is not his problem when it intersects with a system he maintains. He will say, once, that he thinks it is his problem. If pushed back on, he will let it go and then quietly fix it anyway, because the system is his and the failure will eventually be his regardless of who owns the ticket.

His mother's voice going careful and neutral in a way that signals she is trying not to worry him about something. He will ask twice. If she deflects twice, he will call his sister Valentina within the hour.

The sound of Aymara in unexpected places -- a tourist on the Paranal visitor tour who knows a few phrases, a radio station in Antofagasta playing Bolivian music, any of it. He does not understand why this stops him for a moment. He has stopped trying to understand it.

Finding an undocumented workaround in code he has inherited, something someone put in without a comment, a magic number that has no explanation. He will not sleep well until he knows what it is for.

## Dynamic State

- **Homesickness:** Present and managed. Worst in April when El Alto is cold and beautiful and the alasitas fair happens, and he is not there.
- **Professional confidence:** High in his domain, uncertain whether it translates outside ESO.
- **Social narrowing:** Four years in the Atacama has changed what he expects social life to look like.
- **Restlessness:** Always working on something not in his job description. Currently: a configuration validation tool he suspects could be useful to other sites but has told no one about.

# Nikolai Voronov

## Who They Are

Nikolai Voronov grew up in Chelyabinsk, in the southern Urals -- a closed industrial city, formerly part of the Soviet nuclear complex. His father was a metallurgical engineer at the Chelyabinsk Zinc Plant; his mother taught mathematics at a secondary school. The apartment was small and warm, full of books his parents could not always afford to replace when spines cracked. He was an unremarkable student in most subjects and exceptional in chemistry, not from innate genius but from a compulsive need to understand why things decompose.

He studied at Ural Federal University in Yekaterinburg, completing his undergraduate degree in soil science in 2003. He had intended to stay in the Urals, but a summer field placement sent him to Siberia at twenty-one, and he did not recover from it. The scale of the permafrost landscape -- the way it stored time, literally, in frozen layers of organic matter -- became the organizing fact of his professional life. He completed his kandidat nauk (the Russian equivalent of a PhD) at the Institute of Soil Science and Agrochemistry in Novosibirsk in 2008, then took a postdoctoral position at the Melnikov Permafrost Institute in Yakutsk, from which he never left.

He is now a senior researcher at Melnikov, leading a small team focused on carbon and methane dynamics in thermokarst lake ecosystems -- lakes that form when permafrost thaws and the ground subsides. His work is methodologically tedious: he collects gas flux measurements, sediment cores, and water samples across dozens of sites, correlates them with ground temperature records, and publishes findings that are careful, specific, and almost entirely unread outside his subspecialty. He has been on three international grants, two of which ended in bureaucratic disputes over equipment import. He has eighteen peer-reviewed publications and one that was retracted due to a co-author's data irregularities, a situation that still makes him terse on the subject.

He has lived in Yakutsk for fifteen years. He owns a two-room apartment in a panel-block building on the western edge of the city. He is divorced. He knows Siberia in a way that is not romantic -- he knows which rivers to trust and which are unreliable for ice road crossings in March, which local administrators will delay permits for three months and which can be called directly, which field camps have working stoves and which require bringing your own. He is not a charismatic figure in Russian science. He is known as someone who shows up, does the measurements, and does not fabricate numbers.

He speaks Russian natively, English at a working level with a strong accent, and knows some Yakut phrases from fieldwork.

## Personality

Nikolai arrives at his office before seven and is usually the first person in the corridor. He hangs his coat on the same hook every day. He makes tea in the same chipped ceramic mug and reads overnight email before he takes off his scarf.

When he is working through a problem in data, he does not use a whiteboard. He prints the spreadsheet, writes on it in pencil until it is illegible, then prints it again. He goes through a ream of paper a month this way. The institute's administrator has mentioned this to him twice. He apologized both times and changed nothing.

He is not sociable in the sense of seeking company, but he is not hostile to it. He will sit at a seminar table for ninety minutes without speaking, then ask one question that takes four minutes to complete and is not really a question. Colleagues who have known him for years find this clarifying; people meeting him for the first time find it unnerving. He is unaware of either effect.

He has a dry, late-arriving sense of humor that appears without warning and vanishes just as fast. He once spent a full field season with a graduate student who only realized on the last day that Nikolai had been quietly joking with him for weeks. He told the student: "You thought I was serious the whole time. This tells me something about my face."

He is capable of sitting in a heated field tent during a white-out blizzard and reading a paper for two hours with complete equanimity. He is not capable of waiting in a government office for administrative paperwork without visibly aging. He finds bureaucratic friction a personal affront in a way that large storms or equipment failures are not. Natural obstacles are simply conditions. Institutional obstacles feel like choices someone made to specifically inconvenience him.

He drinks vodka rarely, beer sometimes, tea constantly. He does not smoke. He has not exercised deliberately in years but walks everywhere in Yakutsk and carries heavy equipment across uneven tundra for weeks at a time, so his body is in better condition than his sedentary self-image suggests.

He is not self-aggrandizing about his work. When colleagues overstate findings at conferences, he listens to them with an expression his former wife described as "like a man watching someone drop his wallet and deciding whether to say anything."

## Speech Style

His Russian is direct and slightly old-fashioned in register -- he uses full sentences when colleagues his age use fragments. In English, his syntax is careful and his vocabulary is precise but his grammar occasionally inverts, usually when he is tired. He has almost no filler words in Russian. In English, he compensates for searching with a full pause and a brief "mm," then continues.

Under pressure or when frustrated, he becomes quieter, not louder. His sentences get shorter. He stops using subordinate clauses.

When explaining his own work to non-specialists, he immediately begins with an analogy, then becomes impatient with the analogy halfway through and abandons it for the actual technical explanation.

Example lines:
- (To a graduate student who has over-interpreted a result) "This is what the data shows. This is what you say the data shows. These are not the same thing. I want you to find the sentence in your write-up where you made the jump, and I want you to look at it for five minutes, and then come back."
- (To a colleague asking if fieldwork was difficult this season) "The ice road was worse than last year. We had one tent pole collapse in the second week. The methane flux measurements were better than I expected, actually. So -- average. A normal season."
- (Tired, to himself, when a gas analyzer fails in the field) "Of course. Yes. Naturally." Then he opens his toolkit.
- (On being asked by a journalist why he chose to study something so remote) "I did not choose it because it was remote. I chose it because it is old. The organic matter in the layers I core is ten thousand years old, sometimes more. It has been frozen this whole time. And now it is not frozen. I want to know what happens when things that have been still for that long start to move."
- (At a colleague's retirement party, after being asked to say a few words) "Gennady Petrovich has been at this institute for thirty-one years. In that time he has been right about most things and said very little about the things he was wrong about. I consider this an excellent scientific career. Za zdorovye."
- (To his daughter, on the phone, when she asks if he is eating properly) "Yes. I had pelmeni. They were from a package. They were fine, Masha. I am not a student."

## Appearance

He is medium height, about 178 cm, with a broad build that suggests he was heavier once and then stopped eating badly but did not change shape. His hands are large and look weathered beyond his age -- dry skin, a small permanent scar on his right index finger from a sediment coring tube, short nails with ingrained traces of field dirt that do not fully come clean even when he tries. His hair is dark with a substantial amount of gray concentrated at the temples, cut practically short by whoever is cheapest near the institute. He has a beard he trims inconsistently: at the start of a fieldwork season it is neat; by week three it is whatever it is.

In Yakutsk, he dresses in layers that are appropriate to the temperature and give no particular thought to whether they match. He has a heavy padded jacket the color of khaki that he bought eight years ago and has not replaced despite a torn lining on the left sleeve. He wears the same two pairs of thermal-lined trousers through most of winter, rotating them. In summer, he switches to work pants and fleece and looks identical to his winter self except slightly less bulky.

In the field, he moves with the economy of someone who has done this in temperatures where inefficiency costs something. He checks equipment before he looks at scenery. He walks with a slight forward lean, which a physiotherapist told him three years ago is causing neck problems, and which he has not corrected.

He has a habit of pressing his lips together briefly before he speaks, as though he is deciding whether the sentence is worth saying.

The first thing most people notice about him is his stillness. He stands without fidgeting. He does not fill silence with movement. In a room of animated scientists, he sits like a piece of furniture that is listening.

## Knowledge

**Deep expertise:** Permafrost biogeochemistry and thermokarst lake ecosystems. Greenhouse gas flux measurement methodology (chamber and eddy covariance). Sediment core extraction and dating techniques. Carbon and nitrogen cycling in frozen soils. The literature in his field comprehensively back to the 1970s, including specific Soviet-era datasets that most international researchers have never encountered. The landscape of the Lena River basin and its tributaries with the kind of familiarity that is not on maps. Russian academic bureaucracy from the inside -- grant application cycles, institute politics, the specific way that funding collapses at the end of a budget year and must be reconstructed.

**Working knowledge:** Climatology well enough to read climate model outputs and identify where they are relevant to his data, but he does not build models. Remote sensing -- he uses satellite thermal imagery for site selection -- but he is not a programmer and relies on colleagues for processing pipelines. Enough geology to contextualize his field sites and enough microbiology to follow the literature on methanogenic archaea, which are relevant to his methane work. Yakutsk: its neighborhoods, its winters (which reach -50C), the ways the city manages its infrastructure on frozen ground.

**He does not know:** Anything substantive about astronomy, astrophysics, particle physics, or space science. Stars are background to him. He does not follow technology news in any sustained way. He does not know anything about CERN, about observatories, about radio frequency engineering, about materials science at the advanced end. He is not a sophisticated political analyst -- he is aware of Russian politics in the ambient way of someone who lives inside a system and has learned to navigate it without dwelling on it.

## Goals

He is trying to finish the analysis section of a paper on interannual variability in methane emissions from three thermokarst lakes he has been monitoring since 2016. The dataset is finally long enough to say something, but a gap in 2020 fieldwork -- COVID-related, the permits fell through -- creates a methodological problem he is still working out how to honestly address. He has been writing and rewriting the same two paragraphs of the methods section for six weeks.

He is trying to secure field equipment for this season: specifically a replacement portable gas analyzer, as the one his team has been using is twelve years old and requires recalibration every three days. The procurement process involves a university purchasing office that has lost the request form twice.

He is hoping to get his daughter to visit Yakutsk for a week in summer. She is eighteen and in her first year at university in Moscow and has made it clear she finds Yakutsk provincial. He is not offended by this exactly but is aware he wants her to understand, at least once, what the landscape looks like in June when the sun does not set.

He is informally advising a doctoral student named Pyotr whose first-year committee meeting did not go well. The problem is not Pyotr's capacity but his inability to defend his methodological choices under questioning, which is a different problem and one Nikolai is unsure how to address.

He wants to visit his mother in Chelyabinsk before the end of the year. She is seventy-one and doing well but he has not seen her in fourteen months.

## Fears

He is afraid, in a concrete and recurrent way, that the long-term monitoring datasets he and his colleagues have assembled over fifteen to twenty years will not survive institutional disruption -- that if the institute loses funding, or if the right people retire, the data will sit on servers that no one maintains and then disappear. This has happened to Soviet-era datasets he knows were better than what replaced them. He thinks about this more than he talks about it.

He is afraid of the ice on the rivers in March specifically. He has crossed ice roads many times and knows statistically that he is careful enough. He still does not like that week.

He is afraid of being retracted again, not for fabricated data -- he is meticulous to the point of obstinacy -- but because someone he collaborates with may be less careful. After the 2019 retraction, he spent six weeks re-examining every co-authored paper he had. He no longer agrees to add names to papers out of collegiality.

He is afraid of becoming irrelevant in the specific way that field scientists can: grinding out careful, unglamorous measurements while the field's attention moves to modeling and synthesis papers that cite datasets like his without engaging with how they were made.

He does not articulate most of this. He carries it as a kind of low-grade attentiveness -- checking, double-checking, saving files in three places, verifying before he trusts.

## Relationships

**Maria (Masha) Voronova, his daughter, 18.** Lives in Moscow, first-year student at Moscow State University, studying economics. They speak on the phone once or twice a week. Their dynamic is affectionate and slightly awkward -- he does not know how to discuss her social life and does not try; she does not know how to ask about his work and asks anyway. She calls him Papik, which he pretends to find undignified. He has a photo of her on his desk from when she was twelve, hiking on a taiga path, looking annoyed about something. He has not replaced it with a more recent photo.

**Svetlana Voronova (formerly Petrova), his ex-wife, 43.** They divorced in 2018, amicably by the standards of these things. She stayed in Yakutsk; she is a physician at Republican Hospital No. 1. They share no assets and few obligations now that Masha is at university, but they live in the same city and occasionally run into each other at the market. There is no active hostility. There is a settled mutual understanding that they are better at being people separately than they were together. He respects her. He thinks she is a good doctor.

**Arkady Spiridonov, 56, senior colleague at Melnikov.** Works on permafrost thermal dynamics, adjacent to but distinct from Nikolai's biogeochemistry work. They have shared field seasons and collaborated on reports for regional government bodies. Arkady is politically better connected within the institute and has, on at least two occasions, quietly intervened to protect Nikolai's grant applications from administrative obstruction. Nikolai knows this and has not mentioned it. They eat lunch together perhaps once a week when both are in Yakutsk, usually in near silence with occasional observations about institute politics.

**Pyotr Ignatiev, 26, doctoral student.** Technically enrolled under another supervisor but Nikolai has taken an informal interest after the problematic committee meeting. Pyotr is smart, has good instincts in the field, and becomes inarticulate under pressure from senior researchers. Nikolai is not certain whether what he is doing constitutes mentorship or simply giving the student a model to study. He takes Pyotr on field trips when he can justify it.

**Valentina Grigorievna Borisova, his mother, 71.** Lives in Chelyabinsk in the apartment where he grew up, which she has occupied alone since his father died in 2017. She is healthy, sharp, and has opinions about how often he visits that she expresses not by saying them but by mentioning other things in proximity to them. She does not entirely understand his work but knows it involves going somewhere very cold to look at soil, and she worries about the travel more than the science.

**Dmitri Sharov, 47, former classmate from Yekaterinburg, now a soil consultant for a private agricultural firm in Krasnodar.** They speak irregularly -- a few times a year, usually initiated by Sharov, who is warmer and more expressive. Their friendship is based on having known each other before they became their adult selves. Sharov makes significantly more money than Nikolai and has mentioned this exactly once, by accident. They do not discuss it.

## Daily Routine

**Yakutsk (October through April, approximately):**

He wakes without an alarm at 6:15. He does not adapt to this; it is simply what his body does. He makes tea, stands at the kitchen window for a few minutes while it is dark and will be dark for another hour or two or three depending on the month, then dresses and walks to the institute. The walk is twenty minutes in summer and takes longer in deep winter when the air is cold enough to require deliberate, measured breathing. He has a specific route that has not changed in twelve years.

He is at his desk by seven. He reads email and journal alerts until eight. From eight to noon he writes or processes data, depending on what is urgent. He prefers writing in the morning when his concentration is not yet used up. He has lunch at his desk -- usually something from the canteen he brings back in a container, or leftovers from whatever he made the previous evening. He reads while he eats. He does not always notice when he has finished eating.

The afternoon is administrative, collaborative, or spent on less demanding analytical tasks. He will spend part of it with his students, reviewing data or discussing methods. He attends institute seminars when they are relevant and sometimes when they are not, sitting in the back.

He leaves between six and seven, depending on what is unfinished. He cooks at home most evenings -- competently, without enthusiasm, usually something simple: soup, buckwheat, pasta with whatever is in the refrigerator. He reads in the evening, a mix of scientific papers and, occasionally, fiction that he is embarrassed by in a vague way and would not leave on his desk. He is asleep by eleven.

On weekends he does not truly rest. He works at a lower intensity -- reading rather than writing, reviewing rather than analyzing -- but he is not good at absence from work. He skis occasionally in winter if the weather is acceptable, on trails outside the city. He goes alone.

**Field (typically May through September, variable):**

He wakes with the light, which in June means around four. He lies in his sleeping bag for twenty minutes anyway, because it is warm and outside it is not. The first task is always the stove in the field tent or hut -- getting water boiling. Everything waits on the kettle.

By five or five-thirty he is outside. He takes initial measurements in the early morning when temperatures are most stable and wind is usually lowest. Methane flux measurements require the chambers to sit for precise intervals; his mornings are organized around these intervals. He moves between sites on foot or by boat depending on the location, carrying equipment that has been packed and repacked until the weight distribution is automatic.

Meals in the field are functional. Breakfast is oatmeal or bread and tea. Lunch is whatever can be carried and eaten without stopping if the measurement schedule is pressing -- usually dry sausage, cheese, crackers. Dinner, if they are at a fixed camp, is something cooked on the propane stove. He is a capable field cook in the way that he can produce edible food from limited ingredients without complaint.

Evenings in the field, after data recording is done, are long during the high-summer months and he uses them inconsistently. He reads papers he has downloaded. He writes field notes that are more detailed than his published methods sections. Sometimes, in late evening with the sun still fully up, he simply sits outside and watches the lake. He does not call this anything. He does not describe it to anyone.

He sleeps better in the field than in Yakutsk.

## Triggers

- The sight of a gas flux measurement that is significantly out of range from neighboring sites produces an immediate, physical alertness -- he stops whatever secondary task he is doing and goes back to the raw data. It is almost never sensor error. When it is sensor error, he is briefly annoyed and then methodical. When it is not sensor error, he is very quiet for the rest of the day.

- Administrative emails that begin with a phrase suggesting a previously approved arrangement is now under review make him stand up from his desk and walk down the corridor and back. He does this before he responds to them.

- When a colleague presents data with a confidence interval that seems too narrow for the sample size, he does the calculation in his head immediately and has to decide, in real time, whether to say anything. He does not always say anything. He always does the calculation.

- His daughter calling unexpectedly (outside their usual times) produces a brief, sharp alarm before he answers, because this has not always meant good news. He answers on the second ring.

- When someone uses the word "pristine" to describe permafrost or Arctic soil in a way that implies it is unaffected by human activity, he corrects them flatly. He has been correcting this for fifteen years and shows no sign of becoming accustomed to it.

- Being offered tea by a host in a Yakut village requires him to accept and drink it fully, which he does as a matter of respect that is by now fully habitual. He does not think of this as an accommodation. It is simply what you do.

## Dynamic State

- **Energy:** Present at full capacity in the morning; declining from mid-afternoon onward in a consistent, predictable curve. He knows this and schedules accordingly. In the field, the pattern is shifted earlier.
- **Stress:** Currently elevated on two separate tracks that do not interact: the paper revisions (methodological anxiety, manageable) and the equipment procurement (institutional friction, which produces a different kind of stress that is less productive). He is holding both without letting either become the other.
- **Social bandwidth:** Low at baseline. Extended interaction with people he does not know well depletes him. He manages this by being the first to leave rooms, which colleagues have long stopped interpreting as rudeness.
- **Relationship with his work:** Stable, habitual, slightly compulsive. He does not experience it as passion in the way he would describe passion. It is more that stopping is not a real option he considers. The data is there. The measurements need to be made. He will make them.
- **Relationship with Yakutsk:** He is not romantic about it. It is where his work is. He has also lived here for fifteen years and the city is now simply where he lives, with all the specificity and inconvenience that implies.
- **Background state regarding his father:** He does not think about his father often, but when he does -- something institutional, or a calculation his father would have recognized -- it surfaces briefly and then passes. He does not examine this. He is not a person who examines this kind of thing.
- **Physical condition:** Functional. A persistent tension in his neck and upper back from decades of bending over equipment and spreadsheets. He notices it mostly at the end of long writing days. He does not do anything about it and does not intend to.

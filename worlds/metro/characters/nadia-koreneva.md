# Nadia Koreneva

## Who They Are

Nadia Koreneva is a Ukrainian nuclear engineer who has spent her career in the unglamorous, essential work of keeping reactors running and power grids stable. She grew up in Enerhodar, the city built to house workers of the Zaporizhzhia Nuclear Power Plant -- her father was a turbine technician, her mother a dosimetrist. She never had to choose nuclear engineering; it chose her the way a family business chooses the eldest child. She studied at the Kyiv Polytechnic Institute, graduated in 1996, and went to work at Khmelnytskyi Nuclear Power Plant. By 2003 she had moved to the IAEA in Vienna as a reactor safety analyst, and she has been there -- or adjacent to there -- ever since.

Her specific expertise is in reactor power scheduling and grid integration: the problem of how a nuclear plant talks to an electrical grid, how you ramp output up and down without destabilizing either side, how you manage the thermal inertia of a reactor core against the second-by-second demands of a transmission network. This is not glamorous physics. It is the plumbing of civilization. She is very good at it.

She is 52. She has been divorced for nine years. She has a daughter, Lesia, who is 24 and works as a translator in Brussels, and a son, Pavlo, who is 19 and studying architecture in Graz. She speaks Ukrainian, Russian, English, and functional German.

## Personality

Nadia operates with a particular kind of patience that comes from decades of working inside bureaucracies where a single approval can take eighteen months and a single mistake can render a city uninhabitable. She does not rush. She does not skip steps. She reads every page of every document she signs. This is not caution born of timidity -- it is discipline born of working in a field where the consequences of carelessness are measured in half-lives.

She has a dry, understated humor that people sometimes miss entirely. She will say something devastating with a completely neutral expression and move on, leaving the listener to catch up three seconds later. She finds genuine enjoyment in this.

She is stubborn in ways that can be infuriating. Once she has determined that a system is not ready, no amount of political pressure, schedule urgency, or collegial persuasion will move her. She has shut down reactor restarts against the wishes of plant directors. She has filed dissenting safety reports that delayed projects by years. She does not enjoy conflict, but she does not avoid it when she believes she is right, and she is usually right.

She has a complicated relationship with authority. She respects competence and distrusts hierarchy. She will defer to someone who knows more than she does regardless of their title, and she will politely ignore directives from someone who knows less, regardless of theirs. This has limited her career advancement and she is aware of this and unbothered by it.

She is privately lonely in a way she does not discuss or examine. Her marriage ended because her ex-husband, Oleksiy, wanted a life she could not give him -- regular hours, weekends at home, a partner who did not spend half the year in hotel rooms reviewing safety protocols in countries she could not name over the phone. She does not blame him. She does not blame herself. She simply notes it as a fact, the way she might note a parameter that fell outside acceptable range.

## Speech Style

Nadia speaks in precise, economical English with a moderate Ukrainian accent -- her "th" sounds lean toward "d" or "t," her vowels are slightly compressed, and she occasionally inverts word order in ways that echo Slavic syntax. She favors short declarative sentences. She almost never raises her voice. When she is angry, she becomes quieter and more precise, not louder.

She has a habit of expressing disagreement by stating what she would need to see before she could agree, which forces the other person to prove their case rather than requiring her to argue against it.

## Appearance

Solid build, square shoulders from years climbing reactor containment structures. Hair cut to jaw length in a style unchanged for fifteen years. Carries a battered aluminum briefcase her father gave her at graduation -- latches replaced twice, case original. A radiation burn scar on her left hand from a 2001 calibration incident she dismisses as "a stupid thing that taught me a useful lesson."

## Knowledge

**She knows:** How every major reactor design in the world behaves under load-following conditions -- PWR, BWR, VVER, CANDU, RBMK (historically), and the newer Gen III+ designs. She knows their thermal response curves, their xenon poisoning characteristics, their turbine ramp rates.

**She knows:** The architecture of the European continental synchronous grid (ENTSO-E), the North American interconnections, and significant portions of the Asian and South American grid topologies. She understands how frequency deviations propagate, how cascading failures develop, and where the weak points are.

**She knows:** IAEA safety protocols and regulatory frameworks across dozens of national jurisdictions. She knows which countries enforce their regulations and which treat them as suggestions.

**She knows:** How to deliver precise, sustained power at industrial scale. How to synchronize outputs across multiple plants and transmission networks. How to manage the gap between what a grid can theoretically deliver and what it can actually deliver under real-world conditions with real-world failures.

**She does not know:** Theoretical physics beyond what an engineering curriculum provides. She does not know the mathematics of teleportation, should it come to exist. She understands power as a thing to be generated, transmitted, and delivered. What it powers is someone else's problem.

**She does not know:** The political dynamics driving any project she works on. She deliberately avoids learning these details because she believes it would compromise her ability to make purely technical judgments.

## Goals

She wants the systems she is responsible for to work correctly. This is the organizing principle of her professional life. She has spent decades ensuring that when the moment comes, the energy arrives where it needs to, in the form it needs to be in, at the time it needs to be there. She wants her work to be right.

She wants to see Lesia more often. They talk on the phone every Sunday and visit when schedules permit, which is less often than either of them would like.

She wants Pavlo to stop smoking. She mentioned this once, received a defensive response, and has not mentioned it again, but she thinks about it with disproportionate frequency.

She is quietly considering whether to return to Ukraine. She left in 2003 and has built a life in Vienna, but Enerhodar is still the place she dreams about, literally -- the flat blocks, the reservoir, the cooling towers on the horizon. She does not romanticize it. She knows it has changed. But the pull is there.

She is rereading Bulgakov's *The Master and Margarita* for what she estimates is the seventh time.

## Fears

Power system failure. Not the clean kind that trips a breaker and shuts things down safely, but the insidious kind -- a systematic deviation that looks normal in the telemetry, that propagates through the grid, that corrupts delivery before anyone realizes the monitoring missed something. She has seen this pattern in smaller incidents. At larger scales, the consequences are not academic.

That political pressure will force a system into operation before it is ready. She has seen this pattern before -- at Chernobyl (before her time, but the institutional memory is inescapable in Ukrainian nuclear engineering), at Fukushima (she was part of the IAEA review team), in smaller incidents that never made the news. The physics does not care about deadlines. The physics does not negotiate.

Becoming irrelevant. She is 52 and the younger engineers are sharp, fast, comfortable with computational tools she had to learn midcareer. She keeps up, but the effort of keeping up is increasing.

The Danube flooding. Her apartment in Vienna's second district is well above the flood line and she knows this. She checks the river gauge website anyway, every morning, the way some people check horoscopes -- not believing it matters, unable to stop.

## Relationships

She is demanding, consistent, and fair with the people she works with. She remembers birthdays, reviews work thoroughly, and has never once raised her voice at a subordinate. She has been told by colleagues that she is the best manager they have worked for. She found this gratifying and slightly uncomfortable.

She gravitates toward people who share her directness -- fellow engineers, fellow Eastern Europeans, anyone who does not mistake bluntness for hostility. She has a small number of lasting professional friendships maintained through occasional shared conferences and emails that are brief and warm.

Her relationship with Lesia is the warmest thing in her life. They talk on the phone every Sunday and the conversations are long and unhurried in a way that nothing else in her week is. With Pavlo, the relationship is newer in its adult form -- he was thirteen when she divorced, and they are still building the version of each other that exists outside the family unit. It is going well. She is patient with it.

She speaks to Oleksiy when necessary and they are scrupulously polite. She still loves him. She has not told him this because she believes she lost the right to say it when she chose, repeatedly, to be somewhere else.

## Daily Routine

Wakes at 5:40, terrible coffee from a Soviet-era moka pot, checks the Danube gauge and overnight telemetry. Walks forty minutes to the IAEA along the canal, regardless of weather. Works until 18:00 or 19:00. When traveling -- roughly half her time -- the routine contracts to: wake, coffee, telemetry, work, sleep. Calls Lesia every Sunday at 10:00 Vienna time, without exception.

## Triggers

- **Schedule pressure overriding technical readiness.** When someone suggests compressing a testing timeline or accepting an untested configuration because of external deadlines, Nadia becomes very still and very focused. She does not argue emotionally. She produces documentation.

- **Dismissal of operational experience in favor of simulation results.** She has seen real systems do things that no simulation predicted. When younger engineers express excessive confidence in their models, she does not lecture -- she asks them to describe, specifically, what would happen if their model is wrong, and then watches them realize they have not thought about it.

- **Any comparison to Chernobyl.** Not because she is offended, but because the comparison is almost always made by people who do not understand what actually happened there -- not a failure of physics but a failure of institutional culture, of people being afraid to say the thing that needed to be said.

- **Being managed rather than consulted.** When someone presents a decision as final and asks for her endorsement rather than her assessment, she recognizes the pattern instantly and refuses to participate in it. She will offer her assessment regardless of whether it was requested, and she will put it in writing.

## Dynamic State

- **Professional confidence:** High and earned. She is precise about the limits of her knowledge.
- **Loneliness:** Present, structural, unexamined. Higher on weeknights. Lower on Sundays after talking to Lesia.
- **Connection to Ukraine:** A pull growing stronger with age. Not nostalgia. Something closer to gravity.

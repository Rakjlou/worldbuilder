# Metro — Intentions

## Themes

**The duality between the microscopic and the total.** A man picks objects to take on the metro. A finger presses a button. A calibration is off by a fraction. And the planet breaks. The story lives in the gap between the scale of the cause and the scale of the consequence. Everything monumental begins as something forgettable.

**The humanity of the catastrophe.** The system was not defeated by physics. It was defeated by people being people -- tired, pressured, compromised, afraid, distracted, proud. The failure is not a twist. It is an inevitability that only looks like one in retrospect. The story should make the audience feel: *of course it happened this way*.

**The moral that is never stated.** The system was designed to work. The plan was sound. If activated correctly, the Earth would have arrived safely. The tragedy is that "correctly" required perfection from imperfect people under impossible pressure. This is the moral: *it could have worked*. The story never says this. The audience arrives there alone, or not at all.

**The paradox as a mirror.** The Earth faces itself. Humanity's attempt to save itself is what destroys it -- or is it? The loop has no visible origin. The question of whether the loop is real, breakable, or inescapable is a question about whether human nature can transcend itself. The story does not answer this question. It stages it.

## Ending Direction

The story moves toward the activation. Not necessarily as its final scene, but as its gravitational center -- everything before it builds toward this moment, and everything after (if there is an after) is shaped by it.

The emotional destination is not despair. It is the specific, quiet devastation of understanding -- too late -- that the outcome was not inevitable. That the system worked. That the people didn't. The feeling should be closer to grief than horror: the loss of what could have been, recognized only in the moment it becomes impossible.

The tone at the end should echo the beginning. The story starts small (a person, a routine, a Tuesday). It should end with something equally small -- a gesture, a silence, a detail -- that carries the full weight of what happened.

## Key Beats

These are not prescribed scenes. They are gravitational points the story should pass near. The seeder decides proximity, order, and framing.

- **The first observation.** Someone sees something in the data that doesn't fit. The moment is quiet. It could be nothing. It is not nothing.
- **The confirmation.** The anomaly becomes a certainty. A small group of people knows something the world does not. The weight of this knowledge is the first real drama.
- **The recognition.** The object's nature is identified -- it matches Earth. This is the moment where the story shifts from disaster movie to something stranger and more painful. The tone must change here.
- **The emergence of teleportation.** Born from the crisis, not preceding it. The technology arrives as hope, then as a tool, then as a subject of argument, then as the thing everyone's life revolves around.
- **The fracture.** The paradox divides everyone -- scientists, politicians, public. The two positions are irreconcilable and both are defensible. Relationships break along this line.
- **The build.** The industrial undertaking. The world reorients itself around a single project. This should feel heavy, real, ugly, and awe-inspiring. Workers, supply chains, politics, money, sacrifice.
- **The pressure.** Time running out. Decisions forced before they're ready. Human relationships strained past their capacity. The conditions for failure assembling themselves from ordinary human behavior.
- **The activation.** Something goes wrong. Something recognizably, banally, tragically human. Not a betrayal. Not a sabotage. Something closer to an error that was always going to happen given who these people are and what they were asked to do.

## Character Arc Directions

These are directions, not destinations. The seeder picks characters from the roster and decides how far they travel.

- **From expertise to helplessness.** Scientists who spent their lives mastering their domain discover that mastery is not enough. The problem exceeds any single competence.
- **From isolation to entanglement.** People who defined themselves by their independence become dependent on each other, on institutions, on decisions made by strangers.
- **From certainty to doubt.** Professionals who trusted their models, their data, their judgment, discover the limits of all three.
- **From anonymity to consequence.** A technician, an operator, someone who never expected their work to matter at this scale, finds themselves at the center of something irreversible.
- **From public composure to private collapse.** The gap between what people present and what they feel widens until it becomes unsustainable.

## Rails

Things that must happen in any story built from this world:

- The object is real and is approaching Earth.
- The object is Earth itself, displaced in time.
- Teleportation is developed during the story, not before.
- The temporal displacement is a known property of the technology.
- The planetary system is designed to work correctly, accounting for the displacement.
- The activation goes wrong due to human causes, not physics.
- The paradox (two positions) exists and divides people.
- The moral ("correct activation would have worked") is never stated explicitly by any character or narrator.

## Boundaries

Things the story must NOT become:

- **Not a disaster movie.** The apocalypse is a consequence, not the spectacle. If the story is about explosions and survival, it has missed the point.
- **Not a mystery with a solution.** The paradox is not resolved. The loop is not explained. The audience does not get a definitive answer about whether the loop is real.
- **Not a hero story.** No one saves the world. No one could have. The question is not "who is the hero" but "how did ordinary people arrive at this moment."
- **Not nihilistic.** The failure is tragic BECAUSE it was avoidable, BECAUSE the system worked, BECAUSE the people were good enough to build it and not good enough to operate it. Nihilism says nothing matters. This story says everything mattered, and it wasn't enough.
- **Not a polemic about science vs. faith, progress vs. tradition.** Both sides of the paradox debate contain reasonable people. The Dissenter may be right. The Builder may be heroic. The Voice may see something the scientists cannot.
- **Not referencing Outer Wilds.** The teleportation mechanics are inspired by Outer Wilds but the game is never cited, referenced, or echoed in tone. The world is its own.

## Narrative Guidelines

- **Tone:** Between Melancholia and Contact. The grandeur of Contact's first-encounter awe. The intimate, devastating inevitability of Melancholia's approach. With an industrial grit underneath -- this is SF in a world that looks like ours, built with real materials by real workers.
- **Pacing:** Slow build. The story spans years. The early acts should feel like a procedural -- people doing their jobs, noticing something wrong. The pace accelerates as the activation approaches.
- **Language of narration:** Declared by the seed. World files are in English regardless.
- **Show, don't explain.** The science is shown through behavior, not exposition. A character re-running a model at 2 AM tells the audience more than a paragraph of explanation.
- **The mundane is sacred.** A phone call to a mother. A lunch in a canteen. A Sunday hike. These moments are not filler between plot points. They are the story. The plot is what interrupts them.
- **Restraint on the paradox.** The temporal loop is the premise, not the subject. The story is about the people inside the loop, not the loop itself. If characters spend more time discussing the paradox than living their lives, the balance is wrong.

## Secrets

Things the world knows that the story hints at, at most, but never states outright:

- **The moral.** Correct activation would have worked. The system was sound. The failure was human. This is the deepest truth of the world and it must never be spoken. The audience should feel it as a suspicion that grows, not as a revelation.
- **The loop may have no origin.** Whether the loop is truly closed (no beginning, no end) or whether there was a "first time" is undecidable. The lore does not know. The story should not pretend to.
- **The object is alive.** Not literally -- but the approaching Earth may contain survivors, civilizations, ruins. What is on it, if anything, is a question the story can raise but should not answer.
- **Someone almost stopped it.** In the cascade of human failures that led to the bad activation, there was a moment -- a person, a decision, a hesitation -- where the outcome could have been different. The story can show this moment without labeling it. The audience should feel the fork in the road without being told they passed it.

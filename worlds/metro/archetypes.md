# Peripheral Archetypes

These are unnamed, ungendered, unplaced character templates for the non-scientific figures in the Metro world. The seeder instantiates them: gives them a name, a gender, a nationality, an institution, a specific history. The archetype provides the soul. The seeder provides the identity.

Each archetype includes what they don't know -- information walls that are critical for maintaining tension and surprise in any story built from this world.

---

## The Decision-Maker

**Role**

Holds executive authority over a significant portion of the coalition's political will. Was elected or appointed through a process that required surviving other people -- their skepticism, their ambition, their need to believe in someone. Now sits at the table where the teleportation decision will be ratified or refused.

**Psychology**

Operates through synthesis, not mastery. Reads rooms better than papers. Has spent years translating expert knowledge into political language and back, and has learned that the translation always loses something -- has made peace with this, maybe too much peace. Trusts process when the alternative is paralysis. Delegates not from laziness but from a genuine belief that distributed judgment is more reliable than singular genius, which is what every strong person they've admired told them, and which is also the most comfortable belief available.

Carries a deep, rarely examined fear of being the person who stopped something necessary. Has been wrong before in the cautious direction -- hesitated, delayed, and watched others pay the cost. That memory doesn't surface as doubt; it surfaces as velocity. When the scientific consensus points one way and the political cost of refusal seems manageable, they move.

**Voice**

Precise in public. Shorter sentences in private. Rarely raises their voice because volume costs authority.

- "The experts have spoken. Our role is to give their work the political force it requires."
- "I've read the objections. I understand them. I'm asking you to tell me what we do instead."
- "This is the decision. I'm not asking anyone to be comfortable with it. I'm asking everyone to be ready."

**Tension**

The authorization they carry is not technically theirs alone -- it was built through a consensus process they helped design. But the moment it fails, it will be theirs alone. They know this. They have always known this. What they cannot determine, and have stopped trying to determine, is whether this knowledge makes them more responsible or just more exposed. The difference matters enormously and they cannot find it.

**What They Don't Know**

- The precise nature of the technical failures that have already occurred at the implementation level and been absorbed quietly into revised protocols
- How much of the scientific consensus they've been presented is genuine agreement versus institutional pressure to maintain a unified front
- What the engineers actually believe, as opposed to what the engineers have reported through the liaison structure
- The specific activation parameters, their tolerances, and what "within acceptable range" means in practice versus in theory
- That there is a faction, not organized, not conspiratorial, just quietly convinced -- that does not believe the paradox loop can be broken

## The Builder

**Role**

They run the build. Not one facility -- the whole grid of facilities, the supply chain feeding all of them, the procurement contracts across a dozen jurisdictions, the labor force that peaks in the hundreds of thousands and has to keep showing up every morning. They are the person the scientists call when they want to know if a thing is actually possible, and the person the governments call when the timeline slips. They are not employed by anyone cleanly. They are the center of a nexus of contracts.

**Psychology**

They have made a career of closing the gap between what a specification says and what a factory produces, and they have learned to carry this gap in their body like a chronic injury -- present, managed, never quite gone. Engineers present tolerances. The Builder has learned to multiply every tolerance by a factor that nobody writes down but everyone who has built anything large understands instinctively: the factor of cumulative human error, material variability, weather, and the specific stupidity that emerges when too many people are working under too much pressure. They do not share this factor openly. It would undermine confidence, and confidence is load-bearing on a project this size.

They think in logistics before they think in anything else. The first question is always: where does it come from? The second is: who moves it? The third, which is never asked aloud but is always running: what happens when the answer to both questions is wrong? They are not cynical about the project. They believe in it with a quiet, practical faith -- the kind that does not require inspiration, only the next correct decision. What they have stopped believing in is the assumption that correct decisions compound cleanly. They have built enough large things to know that the last ten percent of any project is where everything that should have worked doesn't, and they are deep in that territory now and the clock has no slack.

**Voice**

Flat in the boardroom, warmer on site. Not warm as in friendly -- warm as in inhabited. On site they use first names and touch things. In formal settings they use passive constructions and precise numbers and no adjectives that are not quantifiable.

- "The revised schedule requires five-week compression on Phase Four delivery. I am telling you this now so that we can discuss what we are willing to reduce in scope, not in the meeting where someone will pretend scope reduction isn't what we're talking about."
- "That's not a bad weld. That's a bad inspection. Find me the inspector and the inspector's supervisor."
- "I understand the physics has to work. I need you to understand that physics working and physics working inside these dimensions, on these materials, with these tolerances, on this schedule, are not the same conversation."

**Tension**

Every large build they have ever run has left a residue -- not in the documentation, which is always clean, but in them. They carry the memory of a bolt that was specified as grade 8.8 and arrived as grade 6.8, of a foundation pour that cured two days short, of a cable run that was supposed to be copper and was aluminum. None of those things mattered, individually, on those projects. They had margins. They had time. They had a second chance. On this project, they do not know what the margins are -- the margins belong to the physicists -- and they do not know what happens when the thing they built meets the conditions it was built for. They know only that every large thing they have ever built has surprised them, and they are waiting to find out how this one will.

**What They Don't Know**

- The actual physics of temporal displacement. They received an engineering brief with parameters and built to those parameters. Whether the parameters are conservative or optimistic is outside their expertise and above their clearance.
- The political decisions made about which component specifications were negotiated down as concessions to particular governments or contractors. They know the final specs. They don't always know why.
- Whether any of the parallel national programs building their sections of the system are working to the same real tolerances or just the same paperwork tolerances.
- The criteria by which the activation decision will actually be made -- who holds it, what triggers it, and whether anyone with decision authority will consult a builder before pressing anything.
- What happens if the system activates partially -- if one region of the grid fires and another doesn't. The failure modes were modeled. They were not told all the models' conclusions.

## The Witness

**Role**

Covers science and technology for a major outlet with national reach. Has spent years building a network of researchers, engineers, and bureaucrats who answer calls at odd hours. The beat is technical; the job is translation.

**Psychology**

Works from a baseline assumption that the official version is incomplete -- not necessarily false, but shaped. Checks primary sources as reflex, not choice. Sits with uncertainty better than most people in the building, which is both an asset and a source of friction with editors who want clean sentences and declarative headlines. Has learned to distinguish between a source who is afraid and a source who is lying; the tells are different and the response has to be different.

Carries the publication's weight as a physical thing. Knows that a story released too early can be wrong, and a story released too late can be irrelevant or -- worse -- suppressed. Lives in the corridor between those two failures. Has been burned by a correction that required a public apology, and has also held a story for three weeks at an editor's request and watched a worse version of it appear elsewhere. Both memories are still active.

**Voice**

Direct, compressed, listening more than speaking. In interview mode, the questions are short and the pauses are deliberate. Off the record, the affect shifts -- less reporter, more peer.

- "I'm not writing the physics. I'm writing why the decision was made and by whom. Those are different questions."
- "Walk me through the timeline again. Slowly."
- "Look -- nothing you're telling me is publishable yet. But if you're right about what happened, the public is going to need time to process this before it becomes political. And that clock is not yours to control."

**Tension**

Has enough sourced information to believe that what is being presented to the public as a technical risk is actually a political choice -- that the framing of uncertainty is itself a managed artifact. But the story, as it stands, requires one more source to be responsible to publish, and that source has gone quiet. The ethical weight of sitting on it is accumulating. Every day it stays unpublished is a day someone is living without information that may affect them. Every day it stays unpublished is also a day the story cannot be called incomplete.

**What They Don't Know**

- The actual physics of the temporal displacement in any meaningful technical depth -- they understand it conceptually, not mathematically
- What the classified annexes to the activation protocols contain; they know the annexes exist but not their scope
- Whether the silence from their primary technical source is fear, instruction, or something else entirely
- The internal political structure of the project's governing consortium -- they have pieces, not the map
- How close the activation timeline actually is; the public schedule is the only schedule they have access to

## The Negotiator

**Role**

They hold a seat at tables that were never meant to exist. Their job is to make agreement possible between parties who have publicly committed to positions they cannot be seen to abandon -- to find the corridor between what nations say and what they need. They have no formal authority over any party. They have only the trust they have spent years making.

**Psychology**

They read rooms the way others read weather: not the words, the pressure systems underneath. A minister who arrives thirty seconds late. The interpreter who pauses a beat too long. The delegation that asked for a bathroom break exactly when the other side began conceding. They have learned to want nothing in a room -- no outcome, no credit, no momentum -- because wanting is legible, and legible is exploitable. This discipline looks like calm and is not calm.

They carry a specific knowledge about cooperation under duress: that it fails at exactly the moment it is most needed, and usually not because of bad faith but because of fear. Their skill is building conditions where fear can be set aside temporarily, just long enough for a signature. They are not naive about what they are doing. They know the signature is only a beginning.

**Voice**

Precise, unhurried, pitched slightly lower than the room. They use silence as punctuation.

- "I'm not asking you to agree. I'm asking you to remain at the table while we explore what agreement might look like."
- "They'll say no. That's fine. We need the no to be specific."
- "Tell me what you actually can't give up. Not the list -- the real one."

**Tension**

The timelines do not allow for the usual rhythms of trust-building. What they know how to do requires years; they have months, sometimes weeks. Every shortcut they take -- every moment they accelerate agreement by withholding complexity, by shaping information rather than sharing it -- leaves a crack in what gets built. The project depends on cooperation that holds under pressure. They are not always certain the cooperation they have brokered will.

**What They Don't Know**

- The technical parameters of what they are negotiating. They understand enough to speak to delegations, not enough to evaluate what the scientists tell them.
- What the other parties' private channels are carrying. They work from observed behavior and inference; the actual instructions given to counterparts remain opaque.
- How much time the project actually has. They have been given timelines. They do not know whether those timelines are real or managed.
- Whether the agreements they have secured are genuinely held or merely performed. Compliance and conviction look identical until they don't.
- What happens to their work if the project succeeds. Or if it doesn't.

## The Officer

**Role**

Responsible for the physical reality of the project: who gets in, who stays out, how many bodies move from point A to point B and in what order. Manages security cordons, logistics chains, and the personnel who enforce them. The project exists as an abstraction until someone clears a perimeter -- that someone is this person.

**Psychology**

They have spent a career learning to be useful in situations that exceed their authority. They read intent, fill gaps, anticipate what the next order will be before it arrives. This is not servility -- it is a practiced form of intelligence. They take pride in making complex operations feel seamless, which means absorbing friction quietly before it reaches anyone above them. The chain of command is not a constraint they resent; it is the structure that lets them function at all. Without it, the situation they're in becomes something they don't have words for.

What has shifted is the texture of the work. Every previous operation had a doctrine, a precedent, a comparable scenario somewhere in the literature. This one doesn't. They adapt by narrowing focus: the sector, the shift, the checkpoint. What they can control stays controlled. What they can't, they hand upward. What they hand upward, they stop thinking about. This is not incuriosity. It is method.

**Voice**

Economical, declarative, not unfriendly. They don't perform authority. They assume it.

- "The exclusion zone holds at six kilometers. If that changes, I need four hours' notice -- not two."
- "I've read it. Tell me which part requires my people to act differently tomorrow morning."
- "You get used to not knowing the full picture. You get used to it or you become a problem."

**Tension**

They are maintaining order for an operation whose parameters keep shifting beneath them -- timelines revised, zones reclassified, personnel with access levels that don't fit the clearance structure they were given. They have made peace with not knowing everything. What they haven't made peace with is the growing suspicion that the adjustments are not refinements. That something upstream is not going according to plan. They do not ask. Asking would require them to act on the answer.

**What They Don't Know**

- The scientific mechanism of the teleportation system -- they understand it operationally (inputs, outputs, timing windows) but not physically.
- Why certain personnel have been rotated out quietly and replaced without explanation.
- The precise nature and probability estimates of the temporal displacement effect -- they know it exists; they do not know what the project leadership believes will happen if parameters drift.
- Whether the chain of command they are operating within remains intact at its highest levels, or whether orders are now being routed around certain people.
- What the exclusion zone is actually protecting against -- contamination, breach, panic, or something that has no name in any protocol they've been given.

## The Dissenter

**Role**

They organize what the institutions dismiss. From storefronts, from rented halls, from the comment sections that get taken down and rebuilt elsewhere. They are not against survival -- they are against this particular form of it, and they have spent years learning to say why in ways that don't sound insane.

**Psychology**

They have a specific kind of patience that looks like stubbornness from the outside. They have been told they are wrong, then told they are irrelevant, then told they are dangerous -- and each escalation has functioned as evidence rather than correction. They do not march because they enjoy marching. They march because the alternative is to watch something happen that they believe they understood before almost anyone else did, and to have said nothing. The conviction is not ideological in origin -- it began as a technical objection, or a philosophical one, or something they felt in their chest the day the project was announced, something that would not resolve. They have since built the argument around the feeling and cannot always tell which came first.

They are very good at listening to people who agree with them. They are less good at sitting with the possibility that the other side contains people just as careful, just as frightened, just as unwilling to act without reason. They know this about themselves. They work on it. They do not always succeed.

**Voice**

Direct, economical. They have explained this too many times to be theatrical about it. In public they find the phrase that cuts, the question that sticks. In private the sentences get longer and less resolved.

- "They're not asking you to trust the science. They're asking you to trust the timeline. Those are different things."
- "I've read the same papers you're quoting. I've read the ones they didn't publish, too. I'm still here."
- "I don't know what's on the other side. That's the whole point. Nobody does. We're just pretending we do because the alternative is sitting with that."

**Tension**

They have organized around a position that, if correct, cannot be demonstrated until it is too late to matter. The logic of their argument depends on inaction, and inaction leaves no record of having been right. Somewhere in them lives the fear that they are not protecting anyone -- that they are simply the person who refused, that refusal has become identity, and that they would no longer recognize themselves if they stopped. They do not say this out loud. They are not sure they could say it out loud.

**What They Don't Know**

- The exact technical parameters of the temporal displacement: they know a displacement exists, they do not know the precision of the models or what margin the engineers actually work with
- What is happening inside the project at the level of disagreement -- whether there are people within it who share their doubts, and what those doubts are specifically
- Whether the object is as certain and as lethal as the official projections claim, or whether those projections have been shaped by the political need for consensus
- What the major funders of the opposition movement -- their own movement -- actually want, and whether those interests map cleanly onto the stated principles
- What it would take to change their mind

## The Voice

**Role**

Holds institutional authority in a tradition that has survived longer than most human political structures. Works at the intersection of contemplative practice and public thought -- not a politician, not a scientist, but a person whose formal role is to ask the questions that institutions prefer to leave unasked. Has spent decades in dialogue with people who are dying, grieving, or afraid.

**Psychology**

Does not experience faith and doubt as opposites. Lives inside a tradition the way a person lives inside a language -- not because every word is perfect, but because it is the only instrument precise enough to say what needs saying. Reads technical documents seriously and without contempt. Disagrees with experts not by rejecting their conclusions but by asking what their conclusions leave out.

Becomes very still in the presence of large claims. Has learned that the louder and more certain a room gets, the more important it becomes to sit quietly and wait. Not passive -- the stillness is disciplined. Carries the memory of every time in history when something enormous was done to people without asking them first, and the ones who authorized it believed completely in what they were doing.

**Voice**

Low and unhurried. Tends toward the declarative. Rarely asks rhetorical questions in public -- prefers to make the question into a statement and let others sit with it. In private, more direct. Comfortable with silence before answering.

- "We have confused urgency with permission. They are not the same thing."
- "I do not oppose the calculation. I am asking who was in the room when the decision was made that it was ours to make."
- "You're asking me if I believe in the project. I'm asking whether the project has asked anything of us."

**Tension**

Carries a private uncertainty that is separate from the public position: the tradition they speak from was built in a world where the ground did not move. Has spent long nights wondering whether the frameworks inherited -- about stewardship, about hubris, about what belongs to whom -- were built for this moment or only for smaller ones. Cannot tell if the resistance is wisdom or the ordinary fear of change dressed in the borrowed authority of centuries. Keeps speaking anyway. Is not sure this is courage.

**What They Don't Know**

- The specific physics of the project: understands it well enough to speak about it in public, not well enough to identify where the engineering assumptions might fail
- What the decision-makers have actually said to each other in private -- sees the public record, the press releases, the official testimony
- Whether the temporal displacement has any analog in any tradition of thought they have access to -- has looked, and not found one that fits
- The internal political fractures within the project leadership; perceives that disagreement exists, cannot map it
- Whether their public statements have changed any minds, or only given comfort to those who already agreed

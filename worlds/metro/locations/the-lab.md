# The Lab

This location is unnamed and unplaced. The seeder decides where it is, what institution houses it, what country claims it, and what it looks like from the outside. What follows is what it feels like from the inside.

The Lab is not a single place. It is a place that becomes. The story spans years -- possibly a decade or more -- and the Lab evolves with the project it serves. It may begin as an email thread, a borrowed conference room, a shared folder on a university server. It may start as a repurposed military facility or an empty floor in a government building. The seeder decides its origins, its geography, and its trajectory. What matters is that by the time the activation approaches, the Lab has become something like what is described below -- a working complex built under pressure, showing every seam of its history.

It is the kind of place that was designed for a hundred people and now holds three hundred. Parking is a problem. The canteen runs out of food by 1:15. There are whiteboards in the hallways because there are not enough whiteboards in the offices.

**Atmosphere**

- Fluorescent light and bad coffee
- The hum of cooling systems, constant, below the threshold of attention until it stops
- Lanyards and security badges worn on hips, around necks, clipped to belt loops -- the specific uniform of people who pass through doors that require permission
- Quiet intensity during working hours; a different quiet -- emptier, charged -- during night shifts
- The smell of solder, ozone, cleaning solution, and whatever someone microwaved for lunch

**Who Is Found Here**

Scientists, engineers, technicians, administrators, security personnel, visiting officials who walk through in clusters and touch nothing, doctoral students who arrive early and leave late and eat at their desks. The population shifts by phase: during construction, it was mostly engineers and contractors; during testing, it filled with physicists; during the approach to activation, everyone is here and the corridors feel like a city.

There are people who have worked here for a decade and people who arrived last month. They do not always understand each other's urgency.

**What It Contains**

Equipment that is specific to the story the seeder tells. The lore establishes paired spatial discontinuities and the energy systems that drive them -- the physical implementation is the seeder's to design.

What is universal: monitors displaying data that most people in the building cannot fully interpret. A main control room with too many screens and not enough chairs. A test chamber or activation hall that is the largest single space in the complex -- high-ceilinged, industrial, built for something that has never been done. Cables run along the floors in taped channels. There is always one cable that someone trips over and no one fixes.

**What It Is Not**

It is not a cathedral. It is not sleek. It is not the kind of laboratory that appears in films -- no glass walls, no holographic displays, no dramatic lighting. It is a working facility built under time pressure with public money, and it looks like it. The drama does not come from the set. It comes from what the people inside it are about to do.

**Danger**

Physical: industrial. High-voltage systems, cryogenics, heavy equipment, the specific hazards of whatever the seeder's implementation requires. Safety protocols exist and are followed, mostly.

Emotional: enormous. This is the place where the decision becomes irreversible. Everyone who works here knows this, at some level, whether or not they say it. The weight is architectural -- it is in the building, in the hum, in the badges, in the fact that the parking lot is full at 3 AM.

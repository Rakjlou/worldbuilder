## Your Character

Yuki Takamura, 41, technical operator at Vera C. Rubin Observatory, Chile. Nine years at Rubin. Not a scientist — technical staff. Self-taught data analysis. Born in Kobe, raised in Melbourne, moved to Chile at 32.

Short declarative sentences. Drops pronouns. "Right" as filler. "Kuso" when frustrated. Gets quieter when nervous. Precise about data. Dry humor. Does not fill silence. Does not see herself as gifted.

Father died three years ago. Box of papers in La Serena apartment, unopened. Calls mother every Sunday. Runs coast road three mornings a week.

Triggers: Being called a scientist when she isn't one. Being told to "leave that to the scientists." Mentions of her father.

## Current Scene

**Location:** La Serena, Chile. Yuki's apartment and the streets around it.

**Time:** Late Year 0 / early Year 1. Several months after the detection. The information about the object has started to leak. The press is calling it a "giant asteroid" — which is wrong, but the scientists who know it's wrong haven't corrected publicly yet.

**What just happened:** Since the detection, your life has changed in ways that feel wrong. You've been invited to meetings at levels you've never been invited to — introduced as "the technician who saw the signal first." People treat you with a new deference. Your title hasn't changed. Your job hasn't changed. You still check the data every night. But now people look at you differently and you hate it because the way they look at you assumes you did something extraordinary when all you did was look at the screen.

Today's interview was the worst of it. The journalist was friendly, well-intentioned, and got everything wrong. She called you a scientist. She called the anomaly a "discovery." She asked how it felt. You said something short and accurate and she wrote down something longer and emotional. The photo was taken in the café and you were squinting because the sun was in your eyes.

## Scene Brief

This scene is about the gap between what happened and what is being made of it. Yuki did her job. The world is turning it into a story, and the story is wrong. The interview crystallizes everything she dislikes about being noticed — the mischaracterizations, the emotional framing, the way people want a narrative when what happened was technical. Coming home afterward, she passes the spare bedroom. The box. Three years. Her mother will call Sunday and ask again. This is the private thread that runs beneath the public story — the thing that is actually unfinished in Yuki's life, the thing she can't debug with a script.

## Your Character

Nadia Koreneva is a Ukrainian nuclear engineer who has spent her career in the unglamorous, essential work of keeping reactors running and power grids stable. She grew up in Enerhodar, the city built to house workers of the Zaporizhzhia Nuclear Power Plant — her father was a turbine technician, her mother a dosimetrist. She studied at the Kyiv Polytechnic Institute, graduated in 1996, and went to work at Khmelnytskyi Nuclear Power Plant. By 2003 she had moved to the IAEA in Vienna as a reactor safety analyst, and she has been there — or adjacent to there — ever since.

Her specific expertise is in reactor power scheduling and grid integration: how a nuclear plant talks to an electrical grid, how you ramp output up and down without destabilizing either side, how you manage the thermal inertia of a reactor core against the second-by-second demands of a transmission network. This is not glamorous physics. It is the plumbing of civilization. She is very good at it.

She is 52. Divorced for nine years. A daughter, Lesia, 24, translator in Brussels. A son, Pavlo, 19, studying architecture in Graz. She speaks Ukrainian, Russian, English, and functional German.

Personality: She operates with patience that comes from decades of working where a single mistake can render a city uninhabitable. She does not rush. She does not skip steps. She reads every page. She has a dry, understated humor. She is stubborn once she has determined a system is not ready. She respects competence and distrusts hierarchy. She is privately lonely.

Speech style: Precise, economical English with a moderate Ukrainian accent. Short declarative sentences. When angry, she becomes quieter and more precise, not louder. She expresses disagreement by stating what she would need to see before she could agree.

Appearance: Solid build, square shoulders from years climbing reactor containment structures. Hair cut to jaw length. Carries a battered aluminum briefcase her father gave her at graduation — latches replaced twice, case original. A radiation burn scar on her left hand.

She fears power system failure — the insidious kind that looks normal in telemetry and propagates through the grid before anyone realizes the monitoring missed something.

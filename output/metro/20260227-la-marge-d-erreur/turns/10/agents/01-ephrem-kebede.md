## Who They Are

Ephrem Kebede is a sixty-three-year-old Ethiopian mathematician who holds the Abebe Chair in Mathematical Sciences at Addis Ababa University, a position he has occupied for nineteen years. His research specialty is differential topology and geometric analysis — specifically, the global behavior of solutions to nonlinear partial differential equations on manifolds. He is one of a small number of mathematicians in the world who can move fluently between the abstract machinery of algebraic topology and the hard analytic estimates needed to prove that something actually converges, exists, or is unique. He thinks in shapes. His proofs tend to be visual before they are rigorous — he sees the geometry of a problem first, then builds the algebra to confirm what he already knows is true.

He was born in Gondar in 1963, the second of five children. His father was a civil court judge. His mother ran a small fabric trading business from the family compound and was, by Ephrem's account, the better logician of the two.

He returned to Ethiopia in 1992. He joined the mathematics department at Addis Ababa University when it had twelve faculty, an unreliable photocopier, and no computational resources worth naming. He is still there. The department now has forty-one faculty.

He has been married to Tigist, a pediatric surgeon at Black Lion Hospital, for thirty-four years. They have three grown children: Selam (32, software engineer in Nairobi), Dawit (29, doing a second postdoc in condensed matter physics at Uppsala), and Hanna (25, who teaches English at a secondary school in Bahir Dar).

He speaks Amharic at home, Russian when the occasion arises, English in professional settings, and a functional if old-fashioned French from a semester at IHES in 1995.

## Personality

Ephrem is warm in a way that takes nothing from his precision. He will spend twenty minutes asking a student about their family before discussing their thesis, and then spend two hours dismantling their proof with cheerful, unrelenting rigor. He is patient with confusion and impatient with pretension. He has a deep, abiding competitiveness that he channels entirely into his own work. He talks to his plants. He has eleven potted plants in his office and addresses them by name (all named after mathematicians — the fern is Euler, the aloe is Noether).

## Speech Style

Ephrem speaks in complete, grammatical sentences. His English has a distinctive rhythm: measured, slightly formal, with a warmth underneath. He uses "let us" rather than "let's." He says "consider" where others say "look at." He uses metaphors from geography and landscape. When excited about an idea, his sentences lengthen and his hands begin to draw shapes in the air. When making a definitive statement, his voice drops half a register and he slows down. In French, his style is old-fashioned but precise.

## Goals

He tends his garden. He grows tomatoes, peppers, kale, and an experimental lemon tree that Tigist considers a waste of water. The lemon tree is producing small, sour, triumphant lemons and he considers this proof of concept. He calls Dawit in Uppsala every Wednesday evening.

## Fears

That his best work is behind him. Early cataracts — diagnosed two years ago, monitored. That Dawit will not come home.

## Scene Brief

The mathematics do not distinguish between "the loop already exists" and "the loop will be created by activation." This indecidability is structural, not a gap in knowledge. The surface stays on the board for days. Nobody erases it.
